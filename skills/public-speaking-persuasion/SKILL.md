## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

**Persuasion vs. manipulation**: persuasion serves both parties' interests; manipulation serves only the influencer's. The **3 Pillars of Persuasion** — Logos (reason, framed from the audience's point of view), Ethos (credibility of the speaker), Pathos (emotion) — must all be present; a message failing to land is missing one of the three. Before drafting anything, define **Point X**: the single active-verb sentence completing "When I finish speaking, my audience will ___." Scale the ask down to an intermediate step if it's too large.

**Reason alone doesn't move people.** Pair logic with emotion — the real question isn't "should I include emotion" but "how boring do I want to be." Most real arguments are won or lost through **logical fallacies**, not merits — learn the catalog (strawman, ad hominem, appeal to authority, false dichotomy, post hoc, cherry picking, etc. — see cheatsheet.md) primarily as self-defense. Fallacies exploit **cognitive biases** (confirmation bias, anchoring, availability heuristic, framing effect) — understanding bias is what separates ethical influence from exploitation of the same mechanisms.

**Storytelling is structure, not talent.** Use the **5-Line Story Structure** (Situation → Desire → Conflict → Change → Result) to plan any story; expand with sensory detail only at the turning point (the 6-Senses framework). Switch to present tense to **relive** a moment instead of **reporting** it. Every story needs 3 ingredients: Specificity, Reliving, Meaning (an explicit bridge sentence connecting the story to its lesson). For short/social formats, use the **Domino-Tile** technique (plant unexplained details, detonate later), swap "and then" for "but/so," and compress to a **60-second hook → progression → conflict → resolution** arc.

**Openings decide everything.** The **Three Openings** — a question, a shocking factoid, or a story (strongest) — always beat a self-introduction. Compress the entire message to the **One-Sentence Rule** (under 8 words, 3 is ideal) before building anything else. Slides should carry images, not text — the **Image-Over-Text Rule** scales text down to zero as audience size grows.

**Delivery is mechanical.** The **5 Aspects of a Powerful Voice** — Breathing, Resonance, Silence, Articulation, Inflection — are trainable in isolation; end key sentences on a downward inflection for conviction. The **4-Step Method**: have something to say → say it well → read the audience → deliver with intensity. Build skill with the **3-Minute Daily Practice Method** (record yourself for 3 minutes, 10 consecutive days).

**Audience diagnosis precedes strategy.** The **4 Types of Audience** — Hostile, Friendly, Apathetic, Uninformed — each need a different opening move. Never argue a hostile audience is wrong; use the **Reopen-the-Decision Technique** (affirm their past choice was right given what they knew, add new information, invite reconsideration). For objections, use **Conversation Aikido**: don't answer the surface claim — reflect it back with "I understand," ask an open question, repeat ~4 times until the real issue surfaces.

**Anxiety is managed, not eliminated.** The **4 Ways of Dealing with Anxiety** ladder (weakest→strongest): remove the source → manage the level → tolerate it → enjoy it (by connecting to a purpose bigger than yourself). Project **soft power** via the **3 Power Tools**: Confidence (project before you feel it), Clarity (compress to 3 reasons), No apologizing.

**Writing is thinking.** If an idea can't be written clearly, it isn't finished. Separate writing from editing completely (Write/Edit Separation — draft fast, revise across 5-9 passes). Start any document with the literal word "This" to force objective-first thinking.

**Pitching leads with the problem, not the solution.** The **7 Elements of an Entrepreneurial Pitch**: Problem, Who, Product, Competition, Make Money, Resources, Capture Value — never open on Product. Climb the **4 Levels of Question** (Fact → Opinion → Impact → Change) to surface real motivation.

**Closing requires emotion, not just logic.** Every close is **Direct** (name the action) or **Indirect** (dramatize the cost of inaction) — pick one. Logic produces agreement; emotion produces commitment. Make inaction structurally harder than acting (**Institutional Yes**).
