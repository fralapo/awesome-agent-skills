# Chapter 12: Sales Pitching & Entrepreneurial Pitches

## Core Idea
A pitch persuades by making the audience feel understood before it offers anything — spend disproportionate time on the problem, not the solution, and treat selling as relationship-building plus disciplined process, not a transaction.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Problem-First Pitching**: what is most obvious to the expert (the solution) is exactly what the audience needs least explained first — they need to see the pitcher understands their problem before they'll listen to a solution.
  - When to use: any sales conversation, investor pitch, or persuasive speech proposing a fix to something.
  - How: describe the pain, the stakes, and the person living it in vivid detail before naming what you're offering. Rushing to benefits/features signals you don't understand the customer.
- **7 Elements of an Entrepreneurial Pitch**: the complete content of a resource-seeking pitch (e.g. a 60-second elevator pitch).
  1. **Problem** — the pain in the world you solve; lead with this, not the solution.
  2. **Who** — the specific individuals who feel this pain and have resources to make it go away.
  3. **Product** — what you actually deliver, named and described.
  4. **Competition** — what alternatives exist; having none is a red flag, not a strength.
  5. **Make Money** — the business model, stated as a concrete unit economic (revenue per unit/visitor/sale).
  6. **Resources** — what you're asking the specific listener for (investment, referral, customer intro) and why they're the one who has it.
  7. **Capture Value** — the asset or return that exists years from now, proving the listener's contribution becomes worth more.
  - When to use: any pitch for resources — investment, partnership, a referral, buy-in for an idea.
  - How: work through all 7 in order; never open on Product/Competition — audiences don't care about the product until they're convinced you understand the problem.
- **4 Habits of Selling**: the recurring behaviors that make selling work, independent of price or pressure.
  1. **Build Relationships** — get out of your own head and into the other person's world before pitching anything; trust precedes any real sale.
  2. **Work the Process** — sales is not linear input-output; sustain the volume of attempts (calls, conversations) regardless of short-term results, using small visible rewards to keep momentum.
  3. **Ask 4 Powerful Questions** (see Levels-of-Question framework below).
  4. **Listen** — minimize talking; use minimal encouragers ("mhm", "tell me more") to let the other person go deeper than they intended.
- **4 Levels of Question**: a ladder for moving a conversation from superficial to motivating.
  1. **Fact** — objective information ("what were your sales last year?").
  2. **Opinion** — the other person's judgment on that fact ("was that good?").
  3. **Impact** — the emotional cost of the status quo ("what's the impact of not fixing that?").
  4. **Change** — what they want different and how.
  - How: move through the levels in order; impact and change questions surface the real motivation a fact question never reaches.

## Key Concepts
- **Elevator pitch**: a complete pitch compressed to about 60 seconds, still containing all core elements.
- **Unit economics**: the concrete dollars/euros made per unit, page visitor, or sale — proof the model works, not just a story.
- **Process goal**: a goal defined by action taken (calls made) rather than outcome (deals closed), used to sustain motivation through variable results.
- **Minimal encourager**: a short verbal signal ("mhm", "tell me more") that keeps a speaker talking without interrupting their train of thought.

## Mental Models
- Think of a pitch as evidence you understand the customer's life, not a features list — the sale is granted once that's believed.
- Use the 7-element structure as a checklist before any resource-seeking conversation; a missing element (usually Competition or Capture Value) is a common reason pitches stall.
- Treat "no" in a sales process as process noise, not signal — sustained volume of quality conversations matters more than any single outcome.

## Anti-patterns
- **Leading with the solution/product**: loses the audience because they haven't yet felt understood; obvious to the expert ≠ obvious to the listener.
- **Claiming no competition**: signals either an untested market or a shallow view of alternatives — investors read it as a warning sign.
- **Selling as pushing/pricing**: treating sales as pressure or discounting skips relationship and process, the actual levers of long-term success.
- **Stopping at fact-level questions**: never surfaces the emotional stakes that actually move a decision.

## Key Takeaways
1. Over-invest in articulating the problem before presenting any solution.
2. A pitch for resources must answer all 7 elements — problem, who, product, competition, money model, resource ask, and captured value.
3. Build the relationship before attempting to sell anything.
4. Sustain sales activity with process-based goals, not outcome-based ones.
5. Climb the 4 levels of questions — fact, opinion, impact, change — to reach real motivation.
6. Listen more than you speak; "tell me more" is a persuasion tool.

## Connects To
- **Persuasion Fundamentals**: problem-first pitching is an application of establishing credibility and empathy before asserting a claim.
- **Calls to Action & Commitment**: the "resources" and "capture value" elements are the pitch's call to action and its payoff framing.
