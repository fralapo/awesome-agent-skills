# Chapter 5: Cognitive Biases & Decision-Making

## Core Idea
Human judgment runs on fast mental shortcuts (heuristics) rather than pure logic. These shortcuts save energy but systematically distort perception, memory, and choice — and anyone who understands them can predict, defend against, or (unethically) exploit them.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **System 1 / System 2 thinking**: fast, intuitive, automatic judgment (System 1) vs. slow, effortful, deliberate reasoning (System 2). Most biases are System 1 taking a shortcut System 2 never checks.
  - When to use: flag any snap judgment on an important decision as "System 1 talking" and force a System 2 pass before committing.
- **Question substitution**: when a hard "target question" (e.g. "how happy am I?") is too effortful to answer, the mind silently swaps in an easier "heuristic question" (e.g. "how do I feel about my dating life right now?") and answers that instead.
  - How: to counter it, explicitly restate the target question before answering and check whether your answer actually addresses it.
- **Prospect theory / framing effect**: identical outcomes are evaluated differently depending on whether they are framed as a gain or a loss relative to a reference point; people are risk-averse for gains and risk-seeking for losses, and losses feel roughly twice as painful as equivalent gains feel good.
  - When to use: frame a request as avoiding a loss, not just gaining a benefit, to increase motivation — and recognize when someone else is using this framing on you.

## Key Concepts (Bias Catalog)
- **Confirmation bias**: actively seeking, believing, and remembering only information that supports an existing belief while dismissing contrary evidence — the master bias behind most motivated reasoning.
- **Consistency bias**: sticking to a past decision or stated position purely to appear consistent, not because it's still correct. Counter by treating consistency as a means, not a goal in itself.
- **Dichotomous (black-and-white) thinking**: collapsing a spectrum of outcomes into two extremes (great/terrible, right/wrong). Overlaps with the false dilemma fallacy when used to manipulate an argument.
- **Peacock bias**: showcasing only successes and strengths while hiding failures and flaws (classic on social media), which makes everyone else's curated life look better than reality.
- **Response bias (non-response bias)**: people who would answer negatively often don't respond at all, skewing surveys, polls, and informal "how did you do" comparisons toward positive results.
- **Projection bias**: assuming others think the way we do, so agreement (e.g. social-media approval) feels like proof we're objectively right, when it's often just an echo chamber (compounds with algorithmic filtering).
- **Negativity bias**: weighting negative information more heavily than positive information of equal size; media exploits this ("bad news is good news" for engagement).
- **Optimism bias**: overestimating good outcomes and underestimating risk, which drives overconfident action (e.g. lottery-style bets) — the mirror image of negativity bias, and also harmful in excess.
- **Representativeness heuristic / "less-is-more" effect (conjunction fallacy)**: adding vivid, coherent detail to a description makes a *specific* scenario feel more probable than a *general* one, even though logically a conjunction of two conditions can never be more probable than either condition alone.
- **Anchoring effect**: an arbitrary, even irrelevant, number seen right before a numeric estimate skews that estimate toward the anchor.
- **Availability heuristic**: judgments about how common or important something is are driven by how easily examples come to mind, not by actual frequency — easily-recalled small numbers can feel more compelling than a valid but hard-to-recall larger set.
- **Halo effect**: the order in which traits about a person are presented shapes the interpretation of every trait that follows; the same list of adjectives produces a more favorable impression when positive traits are listed first.
- **Conformity pressure**: in a group where others visibly give a wrong answer, most people will echo the group's incorrect judgment on an objectively verifiable question rather than break from it — social pressure can override direct sensory evidence.
- **Belief/mindset effect**: what people believe about a stimulus (e.g. "this is high-calorie" vs "this is diet food") changes measurable physiological response to an *identical* stimulus — perception of competence amplifies or weakens actual performance, similar to a placebo effect.
- **Stress-enhanced memory encoding**: brief, moderate emotional/physiological arousal strengthens memory consolidation for the emotionally charged part of an experience specifically — which is why emotionally vivid stories are remembered far better than neutral information.
- **Brain drain (attention residue)**: the mere visible or nearby presence of a phone — even switched off and unused — measurably reduces available cognitive capacity for a demanding task, without the person noticing the effect.
- **Situational power (Lucifer effect)**: ordinary people placed in a strong, deindividuating role-based context (uniforms, anonymity, assigned power) can rapidly escalate to cruelty disproportionate to their personal disposition — context and role can overpower individual character.
- **False memory / reconstructive memory**: memory is not a recording but a reconstruction assembled at the moment of recall from fragments, which is why it can be distorted, merged with later information, or entirely fabricated with high subjective confidence (the "Mandela Effect" is a shared, socially reinforced version of this).
- **Memory implantation / gaslighting**: false memories can be deliberately implanted in a suggestible person through repeated, specific, emotionally-moderate suggestion reinforced by trusted sources and corroborating "witnesses" — the manipulative use of the same mechanism that produces the Mandela Effect and false confessions.

## Mental Models
- Treat every strong gut reaction to a "target question" as suspect — ask whether you actually answered the hard question or a substituted easy one.
- Treat your own memory of an emotionally charged event as a reconstruction, not a recording — corroborate before treating it as fact, especially under legal, journalistic, or high-stakes conditions.
- When persuading, remember people act on the frame (gain vs. loss, first-trait-presented) more than on the underlying facts — control the frame honestly rather than letting it happen by accident.

## Anti-patterns
- **Trusting vivid detail as a proxy for probability**: a specific, coherent story will always feel more likely than a general one, even when it's logically less likely — don't let narrative color override base rates.
- **Assuming social approval equals correctness**: high engagement or agreement on one channel is not evidence of majority truth, only of an audience self-selected by prior belief.
- **Fighting bias with willpower alone**: awareness reduces but does not eliminate bias, because most of it operates unconsciously and below the level decisions actually get made — design environment and process safeguards instead (e.g., remove the phone, restate the target question, check the frame).

## Key Takeaways
1. Biases are shortcuts the brain uses to save energy, not simple stupidity — expect them in yourself as much as in any audience.
2. Framing (gain/loss, first-impression order, anchor numbers) changes the *conclusion* people reach from *identical* facts — control your framing deliberately.
3. Group and authority pressure can override direct evidence in front of someone's own eyes — never assume "obviously true" facts will win against social conformity unassisted.
4. Emotional intensity increases how well something is remembered and believed — this cuts both ways: it strengthens honest storytelling and enables memory manipulation.
5. Environmental cues (a visible phone, a first-listed trait, an arbitrary number) shape judgment without the person noticing — design the environment, don't just rely on willpower.

## Connects To
- **Argumentation & Logical Fallacies**: false dilemma exploits dichotomous thinking; appeal to popularity (bandwagon) exploits projection bias and social proof.
- **Persuasion Fundamentals & Ethics**: understanding bias is what separates ethical influence (working with how people actually decide) from manipulation (exploiting the same mechanisms to mislead).
- **Storytelling Techniques**: emotionally vivid, specific stories are remembered better and judged as more probable — the same mechanism behind the representativeness heuristic and stress-enhanced memory.
