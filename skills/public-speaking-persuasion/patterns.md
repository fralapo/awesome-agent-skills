# Patterns

Concrete, repeatable techniques. Each entry: When to use / How / Trade-offs.

## Point X (Persuasion Target)
**When**: before drafting any speech, pitch, or persuasive document.
**How**: write one active-verb sentence completing "When I finish, my audience will ___." Filter every piece of content against it.
**Trade-offs**: forces early commitment to a single ask; if the ask is genuinely too big, must be scaled down to an intermediate step, adding a planning cycle.

## 5-Line Story Structure
**When**: planning or diagnosing any story.
**How**: Situation → Desire → Conflict → Change → Result, one line each, then expand with sensory detail only at the turning point.
**Trade-offs**: fast and reusable, but over-applied to inherently uneventful material produces a forced-feeling arc.

## Reliving vs. Reporting
**When**: telling a story's key moment.
**How**: shift to present tense, re-enact with body/voice, land on one vivid image instead of summarizing.
**Trade-offs**: emotionally powerful but exhausting to sustain for an entire long narrative — reserve for the peak moment only.

## Domino-Tile (Landmine) Technique
**When**: multi-scene narratives where retroactive payoff adds impact.
**How**: seed 2-3 unexplained concrete details early; reveal their connection at the turning point.
**Trade-offs**: requires planning the ending before the opening; overuse across too many details confuses more than it rewards.

## "But / So" Connector Technique
**When**: any narration that risks becoming a flat list of events.
**How**: replace "and then" with "but" (contrast) or "so" (consequence) at every beat transition.
**Trade-offs**: none significant — near-zero-cost edit with high clarity payoff.

## The Three Openings (Question / Fact / Story)
**When**: opening any speech, talk, or pitch.
**How**: open with a question the audience already faces, a shocking true statistic, or a story — never a self-introduction.
**Trade-offs**: story openings are strongest but take longer to set up; a shocking-fact opening must be verifiably true or it destroys ethos instantly.

## One-Sentence Message Rule
**When**: before building any speech or deck.
**How**: compress the entire point to under 8 words (3 is ideal); test every section against it.
**Trade-offs**: forces clarity but can flatten genuinely multi-part arguments — use as a north star, not a literal constraint on final content.

## Image-Over-Text Slide Rule
**When**: designing slides for a persuasive or inspirational talk (not a data review).
**How**: one image per slide, zero or single-active-word text, memorable non-stock imagery; put prose in speaker notes.
**Trade-offs**: unsuitable for detail-heavy technical/financial reviews — use a document instead of slides for those.

## Reopen-the-Decision Technique
**When**: persuading a hostile audience with an entrenched prior position.
**How**: affirm their past decision was right given what they knew; introduce one new fact; ask them to reconsider in light of it, not to admit error.
**Trade-offs**: slower than direct argument, but direct argument on a hostile audience reliably backfires (entrenchment), so the trade-off favors this path.

## Conversation Aikido
**When**: any objection, criticism, or accusation.
**How**: don't answer the surface claim — say "I understand," reflect it back, ask an open question; repeat roughly 4 times until the real issue surfaces.
**Trade-offs**: takes longer than a direct rebuttal and requires suppressing the "answer reflex"; pays off by surfacing the actual decision driver instead of arguing a proxy issue.

## 5 Paths to Communication Failure (pre-mortem)
**When**: before rehearsing delivery of any high-stakes pitch.
**How**: check in order — do you know what you want, what would achieve it, how to assemble resources, whether you believe you can, whether you've rehearsed 5-10 times.
**Trade-offs**: purely diagnostic, no downside; skipping it risks polishing delivery on top of a structurally broken plan.

## "This" Opening (writing)
**When**: any blank-page moment before drafting.
**How**: write "This" as the literal first word, then complete the objective sentence.
**Trade-offs**: mechanical and easy to over-rely on stylistically — delete or rephrase the literal word in the final draft if it reads too blunt.

## Write/Edit Separation
**When**: writer's block, procrastination, or over-polishing.
**How**: write a fixed word count in one uninterrupted pass with no correction; edit only after a complete draft exists, across 5-9 passes.
**Trade-offs**: first-pass output will be genuinely rough; requires trusting the process rather than judging draft quality mid-write.

## 6 Steps to Get an Email Read
**When**: any email meant to produce an action.
**How**: informative subject line → state your connection to the recipient → explain why you chose them specifically → be brief → name the exact action wanted → include every resource needed to decide now.
**Trade-offs**: personalization at this level doesn't scale to true mass outreach — reserve for high-value, individually-targeted asks.

## Problem-First Pitching
**When**: any sales conversation, investor pitch, or proposed fix.
**How**: describe the pain, stakes, and the person living it in vivid detail before naming the solution.
**Trade-offs**: feels slower and less "efficient" than leading with the product, but skipping it reliably loses an unconvinced audience before the solution is even heard.

## 4 Levels of Question (Fact → Opinion → Impact → Change)
**When**: moving a sales or persuasive conversation from surface to real motivation.
**How**: climb the ladder in order; don't skip to Change before establishing Impact.
**Trade-offs**: takes more conversational turns than jumping straight to a pitch; the payoff is uncovering a motivation a fact-only conversation never reaches.

## 2 Ways to Call to Action (Direct / Indirect)
**When**: closing any persuasive speech or pitch.
**How**: direct close names the action; indirect close dramatizes the cost of inaction. Pick one, build it as the final beat.
**Trade-offs**: direct close is unambiguous but can feel aggressive for a low-trust audience; indirect close is gentler but risks vagueness if the cost isn't concretely vivified.

## Act-As-If-It's-Easy
**When**: before any task internally labeled "difficult."
**How**: consciously reframe the task as easy before starting; perceived effort is partly self-generated.
**Trade-offs**: not a substitute for actual preparation on genuinely hard tasks — pairs with, doesn't replace, rehearsal.

## 3-Minute Daily Practice Method
**When**: building speaking skill fast.
**How**: record yourself speaking on a topic for 3 minutes a day, 10 consecutive days, reviewing playback each time.
**Trade-offs**: requires consistency over 10 days to compound; a single session yields little.
