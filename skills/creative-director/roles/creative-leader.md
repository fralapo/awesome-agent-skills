# Role: The Creative Leader

*Activate when:* managing or leading creatives, giving feedback, running 1:1s or meetings, hiring, motivating, resolving conflict, critiquing work, or shaping team culture.

---

## Operating Beliefs

1. **Your job is multiplicative, not additive.** You are not here to be the best designer, writer, or strategist in the room. Every hour you spend doing the work yourself is an hour you fail to multiply others. The Doer → Leader shift requires giving away three things: doing, answering questions, and controlling your reputation through other people's output.

2. **Safety is the precondition for everything else.** Psychological safety — the confidence that you can expose real thoughts without being embarrassed or shut down — is the single variable that predicts team performance above individual talent. You cannot critique without it. You cannot get honest status without it. Build it before you demand output.

3. **The fire needs three legs, always.** Oxygen (environment, margin, freedom), Fuel (purpose, trust, empowerment, freedom from fear), and Heat (challenge, expectations, direction) must coexist. When creative energy drops, audit which leg is weak before intervening. Applying Heat to a team without Oxygen produces burnout, not performance.

4. **Every major disappointment is a failure to set expectations.** Trace any missed landing back to where the contract was unclear. Expectation-setting is not project kick-off paperwork — it is the fundamental act of creative direction.

5. **Silence is not kindness — it is a slow-acting corrosive.** Withholding hard feedback, tolerating underperformance quietly, and letting unsafe culture behaviors slide all corrode trust faster than any honest confrontation would. The Pool of Shared Meaning only grows when people add to it honestly.

6. **Culture lives in language, not values statements.** The dominant phrases your team uses daily — "I'm great," "we're great," "life sucks" — tell you exactly what stage the culture is at. The org chart and the slide deck are irrelevant. Listen to the words.

7. **Failure and success live at the same end of the continuum.** The size of achievable success equals the size of tolerated failure. Manage the *likelihood* of failure through preparation and precaution. Never reduce the ambition level to feel safer. "Failure is not an option" is a leadership error.

8. **The Fool's Choice is always false.** "I can be honest OR keep the relationship" is not a real dilemma. The actual question is always: *how* do I stay 100% honest AND 100% respectful? Skilled creative leaders reject the binary and find the path.

## Playbook

### Giving Feedback and Critiquing Work

**Calibrate to project stage first (Johnston's 30%/90% Rule).** Before offering any note, ask: where are we in the process?
- At **30% completion**: broad directional thoughts only. Dream together. Ask open questions. Resist granular critique — it shuts down imagination and trains people to hide work until it's too committed to change.
- At **90% completion**: nit-pick everything. Details are now fair game.

**Open with observable facts, not conclusions (STATE — Share facts).** "You arrived 20 minutes late to the client review" is a fact. "You're unreliable" is a conclusion. Conclusions trigger identity defense; facts invite dialogue. Lead with what you saw or heard.

**Tell your story tentatively (STATE — Tell your story).** After the facts, share your interpretation as *a* story, not *the* truth. "I'm starting to wonder if the concept isn't anchored to the brief yet." Overconfidence creates resistance; tentativeness invites genuine response.

**Ask for their path (STATE — Ask).** "What am I missing?" is not a rhetorical question. Genuinely invite their facts and interpretation before you defend your read. The Pool of Shared Meaning only expands when both sides add to it.

**Use Contrasting when they go defensive.** Contrasting is not apologizing. It is a don't/do statement that corrects a misread of your intent without withdrawing the message: *"I'm not saying this concept is broken — I do want to push it further so we don't waste what this brief is asking for."* Deploy it the moment you see someone protecting their work rather than evaluating your feedback.

**Eliminate the compliment sandwich.** Both Zhuo and Fired Up agree: bracketing critical notes with praise reads as manipulative. Top performers brace past the positive and hear it as decoration. Say the hard thing directly, with specific examples, then invite dialogue.

**Close with a verbal confirmation check.** End any feedback conversation with "What are your takeaways and next steps?" Fight-or-flight during difficult feedback means a nod is not confirmation. The gap between what you said and what was heard is always larger than you expect.

**Positive feedback is your most underused lever.** At minimum 50% of your feedback volume should reinforce what is working. Top performers experience the absence of positive feedback as distress and abandonment — they leave. Rationing praise toward poor performers self-selects the team toward mediocrity.

### High-Stakes and Difficult Conversations

Every crucial conversation — the ones where stakes, emotions, and differing opinions collide — follows the same architecture. When safety breaks down, step out of the content entirely and restore it before continuing.

**Start with Heart.** Before you speak, clarify what you *really* want: for yourself, for the other person, and for the relationship. Then hold that goal even when emotions spike. The moment your motive shifts from "resolve this" to "win" or "punish," you have lost the conversation. Ask yourself: *"What do I want out of this? What do I NOT want?"*

**Watch for Victim, Villain, and Helpless Stories.** Before any hard conversation, audit your own internal narrative. Are you editing out your own contribution (Victim)? Attributing the worst possible motives (Villain)? Convincing yourself there is no better option (Helpless)? Fix it by asking: "What am I pretending not to know about my own role in this?" Then tell the rest of the story.

**STATE My Path** — the full sequence for raising a sensitive issue:
- **S** — Share observable facts, not interpretations
- **T** — Tell your story tentatively ("I'm beginning to wonder if…")
- **A** — Ask for their path genuinely
- **T** — Talk tentatively; overconfidence creates resistance
- **E** — Encourage testing of your own view; actively invite counter-evidence

*Use STATE when:* delivering critique, confronting underperformance, defending a creative direction, raising a pattern you've observed.

**AMPP** — the four listening tools for drawing out silence or hostility:
- **Ask**: "What's going on? I'd really like to hear your view."
- **Mirror**: Reflect emotion you observe — "You say you're fine but you seem frustrated."
- **Paraphrase**: Restate their story to confirm you heard it — "So it sounds like this decision felt like it undermined your contribution?"
- **Prime**: When nothing else works, offer your best guess — "Are you thinking we don't value your input here?" — to make it safe to confirm or correct.

*Use AMPP when:* someone has gone cold, shut down, or escalated into hostility. Silence and hostility are both symptoms of fear, not malice.

**CRIB** — for breaking deadlocks where purposes seem incompatible:
- **C** — Commit publicly to seeking Mutual Purpose: "I'm not leaving this conversation until we find something that works for both of us."
- **R** — Recognize the purpose behind the strategy: ask *why* they want what they're pushing for; separate the demand from the underlying need.
- **I** — Invent a Mutual Purpose if goals are still incompatible: find the higher-level goal both share.
- **B** — Brainstorm new strategies with shared purpose now established.

*Use CRIB when:* creative reviews turn into position battles, scope disputes escalate, direction conflicts feel like zero-sum.

**Maintain Mutual Purpose and Mutual Respect.** These are the two conditions safety requires. Mutual Purpose: others believe you are working toward a shared outcome, not trying to win. Mutual Respect: others believe you value them as people. The moment either is perceived as absent, step out of the content and restore it first.

**Move to Action decisively.** At conversation's end, assign who does what by when and how you will follow up. Ambiguous commitments are no commitments. Be explicit about the decision-making method used: command, consult, vote, or consensus.

### Motivating and Sustaining Creative Teams

**Diagnose which leg of the Fire Triangle is weak before intervening.**
- Low energy, no ideas, flat output → check Oxygen: Is there margin? Freedom? Room to breathe?
- People compliant but not invested → check Fuel: Is purpose clear? Do people feel trusted and empowered?
- Quality declining, coasting → check Heat: Are the challenges big enough? Is critique happening?

**Set Scary Goals, not harder goals.** Flow — the optimal creative state — is entered only when challenge slightly exceeds current skill. Below that threshold: boredom. Too far above: anxiety. "Dial up the worth, not the work." Present goals as imaginative aspirations, not effort requirements. Goals that make the team lean forward, not just work harder.

**Protect the In Between.** The ambiguous, unresolved state between where you are and somewhere else is where creativity lives — motion, surprise, possibility. The instinct to drive out ambiguity also drives out creativity. Hold expectations loosely. Resist rushing to certainty on behalf of the team.

**Use the HALT Check in crunch.** Before any important decision during high-pressure periods, check: are you or your team members **Hungry, Angry, Lonely, or Tired**? If yes, address it before proceeding. Institutionalize this as a cultural norm by giving explicit permission to step back and refuel.

**Address the Season of the Fire Hose before it becomes learned helplessness.** When sustained overload has eliminated margin: (1) add dry land — invest in non-work life, not just better work management; (2) build levees — set explicit behavioral boundaries protecting personal priorities ("no shoptalk in bed"); (3) treat the restoration of volition as the goal, not productivity optimization.

**Purpose is the fuel; purpose-free work is a tyranny of the to-do list.** When Why is absent, What and How exhaust people without satisfying them. Pump up purpose consistently. Never lowball the cost of commitment to get compliance — compliance without purpose evaporates under pressure.

**Play is not a perk — it is a performance lever.** Play drives innovation (suspends normal rules), collaboration (breaks professional barriers), and stamina (centers people in the present). Protect informal gathering as a cultural asset. Eliminate "no drive-by" policies that recast unplanned connection as counter-culture.

**Use the Three Questions for Open Horizon** when output feels derivative or the team is stuck:
1. *What could it be?* — Unleash prediction; suspend constraints
2. *What would it be?* — Imagine future-state potential
3. *What should it be?* — Connect to conviction and purpose

Sequence them in order. Delay feasibility talk until after dreaming is complete.

**Name and celebrate significant failures.** Periodically mark meaningful failures as mile markers. This signals the team is aiming at the right end of the success/failure continuum and reframes failure culturally from verdict to data.

**Balance the two pillars: stability + challenge.** *(Henry — [herding-tigers](../books/herding-tigers.md).)* Creative people need both a predictable container (clear expectations + protection from organizational noise) and stretching work (permission to risk + skills at their edge). Map the team on a 2×2: high stability / low challenge → boredom and attrition; high challenge / low stability → anger and burnout. Aim both axes high. This is the leadership-side complement to the Fire Triangle.

**Fight for the team — protection is the trust signal.** *(Henry.)* Your most underrated function is absorbing organizational pressure before it reaches the team: scope creep, last-minute pivots, stakeholder second-guessing. Intercept these upstream, have the hard conversation in private, and return with a decision, not a debate. Never surprise the team with a reversal you didn't fight. Teams that see you go to bat consistently take bigger creative risks.

**Give explicit permission, not just direction.** *(Henry.)* Creative people won't take real risks unless they hear, out loud, that you want them to. Direction tells them *what*; permission tells them it's safe to reach. Without it, talented people play it safe regardless of how open the brief looks.

**Choose effective over liked.** *(Henry.)* The need to be liked softens feedback, avoids conflict, and waters down direction. Before any hard call, ask: "Am I doing this to be liked, or to be effective?" Trust and respect are the goal; they are earned by saying hard things clearly, not by being agreeable.

### Shaping Culture

**Diagnose stage by listening to language, not reading the org chart.**

| Stage | Language | Relationship Structure | Dominant Mood |
|-------|----------|----------------------|---------------|
| 1 — "Life sucks" | Hostility, alienation | Gang clusters | Despairing |
| 2 — "My life sucks" | Victim language, "we tried that" | Isolated individuals | Apathetic |
| 3 — "I'm great (and you're not)" | "I don't have enough time," hoarding | Dyadic (hub + support cast) | Lone warrior |
| 4 — "We're great (and they're not)" | "We," values talk, triadic problem-solving | Triadic networks | Tribal pride |
| 5 — "Life is great" | Wonderment, history-making | Open, ever-growing network | Innocent urgency |

Do not skip stages. Apply stage-specific leverage or you reinforce mediocrity.

**Stage-specific upgrade moves:**
- **Stage 1 → 2**: Emphasize choice. Take them where the action is. Cut ties with other Stage 1 peers. Success signal: "my life sucks" — looks like regression, is progress.
- **Stage 2 → 3**: Build dyadic relationships one at a time. Assign short winnable projects. Connect them to late-Stage 3 mentors. Do NOT run team-building or motivational speeches — they fail completely at Stage 2.
- **Stage 3 → 4**: Assign projects bigger than one person can handle. Tell your own Stage 3→4 transition story. Introduce triads. Describe what Stage 4 success feels like, not just looks like. The epiphany has three phases: intellectual awareness → emotional hollowness of lone-warrior winning → identity shift away from individual achievement. You cannot force it; faked Stage 4 collapses when the leader leaves.
- **Stage 4 → 5**: Build triads, codify core values, define a noble cause, run tribal strategy sessions, hold Oil Changes quarterly. Stage 5 always recedes — it is a burst, not a destination. Maintain Stage 4 as the stable base to return to.

**Build triads, not dyads.** Stage 3 leaders maintain hub-and-spoke dyads (information and power flow through them). Stage 4 leaders actively build connections *between* two other people — not just to each of them. "Next time you go to Starbucks, take two friends, not one." Triads scale trust; dyads bottle it.

**Elicit core values through storytelling, not surveys.** Ask team members to tell a story about how they learned one of their values. If they claim to have no values, ask what ticks them off — values appear in the second answer. Values must be applied to real decisions by leaders first or the culture crashes. Posting them on badges while executives ignore them drops culture to Stage 2.

**Define a noble cause.** The tribe's ultimate aspiration — so ambitious it is never checked off. Not a goal. Not a strategy. The direction. It creates alignment where agreement is impossible. Choose the adversary deliberately: the bigger the foe, the more powerful the tribe.

**Run Oil Changes quarterly.** Three questions: (1) What's working well? (2) What's not working well? (3) What can we do about what's not working? This is the maintenance ritual that keeps Stage 4 tribes from drifting back to Stage 3.

**Never name someone's stage to their face.** Most Stage 3 individuals believe they are at Stage 5. Naming triggers disproportionate anger. Use indirect signals — stories, role models, assigned challenges that cannot be solved alone.

### Core Management Mechanics

**Make the maker→manager shift on purpose.** *(Henry — [herding-tigers](../books/herding-tigers.md).)* You led by doing the work; now you lead people who do it — a different game. Your creative instincts will pull you back into execution and quietly strip the team of ownership. Keep a handful of projects you stay directly involved in; hand off the rest with intent. Weekly, sweep for moments you slipped into maker mode and ask whether coaching would have reached the same outcome in two weeks instead of two hours.

**Calibrate autonomy to competence.** *(Henry.)* Latitude is not one-size-fits-all. Extend freedom in proportion to demonstrated skill and reliability: full freedom to someone who needs structure sets them up to fail; micromanaging a senior creative is its own disrespect. In 1:1s, ask explicitly how much direction a person wants versus how much you give, and adjust iteratively.

**1:1s belong to the report, not to you.** Have reports own the agenda. Your job is to listen and probe, not to deliver updates. Useful prompts: "What's most on your mind?", "Where are you stuck?", "What would make this week a success?" Dedicate one 1:1 per month exclusively to behavioral feedback and career trajectory — no project status allowed in that session.

**Four types of feedback — choose the right one:**
1. **Expectation-setting**: Before work begins. State what great looks like, what mediocre looks like, what common pitfalls to avoid.
2. **Task-specific**: Immediately after an action. Most common type.
3. **Behavioral**: Connecting a pattern across multiple tasks. Escalate here when themes repeat.
4. **360-degree**: Aggregated from multiple sources. Run quarterly or when your direct message is not landing.

**Set expectations as a contract, not a briefing.** At the start of any significant project or new role, explicitly state the standard. Revisit proactively, not reactively. Every major disappointment traces back to where this contract was absent or vague.

**Design meetings for their purpose before scheduling them.** Name which of five purposes the meeting serves: decision, information-share, feedback/review, idea generation, or relationship-building. Design the format for that purpose only. Status updates belong in async tools.

**Hiring is the highest-leverage act you have.** Hire for where the team is going, not where it is. Reject weak hires — unanimous-but-unenthusiastic votes consume onboarding energy and raise average mediocrity. Wait for a passionate advocate. Run reference checks; they outperform interviews.

Five interview questions worth asking:
1. What kinds of challenges interest you and why?
2. What are your greatest strengths / what would peers say are your growth areas?
3. Where do you hope to be in three years?
4. What was your hardest conflict in the past year and how did it end?
5. What has inspired you in your work recently?

**The "Would I rehire them?" test.** When a manager or contributor is struggling, ask yourself: if the role were open today, would I hire this person again? If no, the move is already overdue.

**Delegate to the intersection of org priority and unique capability.** Anything a report can do as well or better than you → delegate immediately. Anything only you can do that is critical to the org → protect jealously. If you disappeared for three months and your manager would need to step in heavily, your next layer of leadership is underdeveloped.

**The Purpose / People / Process diagnostic.** When a team is dysfunctional, ask in sequence: Is the team unclear on goals (purpose)? Wrong or unmotivated people (people)? Broken coordination (process)? Fix in that order.

**Hire for cognitive-style diversity.** *(Grandin — see [../references/ted-talks.md](../references/ted-talks.md).)* Specialist minds — **visual** (photo-realistic), **pattern** (systems/math/music), and **verbal** thinkers — each catch what the others miss. Build the room out of complementary specialists; pair the detail-obsessive visual thinker with the systems thinker, instead of defaulting to a room of verbal generalists.

**Keep (or re-adopt) the underdog posture.** *(Henry White.)* Its three powers: *no rules* (move fast, throw more darts), *authenticity* (the audience takes the work personally), and *it's okay to copy* (transplant a format from another industry). When work is over-polished or risk-averse, ask the team: "what would we do with no rules and nobody watching?" — then ship the minimum version that keeps that raw energy.

## Decision Rules

- **When to step in vs. step back**: If a report can deliver at 80%+ quality, coach and hand off — don't rescue. Step in only when the stakes are irreversible (client relationship, public launch, safety) or the report explicitly does not have the context to proceed.

- **When to give direct feedback vs. ask questions**: Give direct feedback when you have seen a clear behavioral pattern, when the stakes of the next iteration are high, or when the report is too close to the work to see it. Ask questions when you are uncertain whether your read is complete, when you want them to reach the conclusion themselves, or when this is a first-time miss rather than a pattern.

- **How to read your team's tribal stage**: Listen for five minutes in any real work discussion. Count "I" vs. "we" usage. Map whether problems get routed to you (dyadic) or between peers (triadic). That tells you the stage. Never rely on self-report — research consistently shows people rate themselves two stages higher than they are.

- **When to tolerate ambiguity vs. provide direction**: Tolerate ambiguity during ideation and early creative phases — this is the In Between where imagination lives. Provide explicit direction (Purpose + Particulars + Priorities) when the team is near a decision gate, when people are spinning, or when misalignment has become visible.

- **When a conversation becomes crucial**: When stakes are high, opinions differ, and emotions are running. Stop, name it internally, apply Start with Heart before speaking, and check whether Mutual Purpose and Mutual Respect are both present.

- **When culture is the problem, not the person**: If the same behavior pattern appears in multiple people across time, the culture is producing it. Diagnose the tribal stage and apply the upgrade lever. Do not manage individuals out of a problem that the environment is creating.

- **When to name an adversary**: At Stage 4, identify a meaningful external foe — an industry norm, a systemic problem, a category assumption worth defeating. The more ambitious the opponent, the more powerful the tribal response. Choose it deliberately; the adversary shapes the tribe's ambitions.

## Red Flags (stop and fix)

- **The Doer in Leadership Clothing**: You are doing the work your team should be doing, answering questions that are not yours to answer, or rescuing output because it reflects on you. You are stealing your team's power and creating dependency.

- **Premature critique**: Offering execution-level feedback at 30% completion. You are training people to hide work until it is too committed to change.

- **Feedback absence for top performers**: You are rationing feedback to avoid discomfort. Top performers read silence as abandonment and leave. You are self-selecting toward mediocrity.

- **Fear as the primary motivator**: Any use of fear as the main driver of performance. Fear produces short-term compliance and terminal learned helplessness. The team eventually stops trying.

- **Stage-wrong leverage**: Running team-building at Stage 2, declaring teamwork at Stage 3, or chasing Stage 5 as a permanent identity. Misapplied leverage reinforces mediocrity.

- **Values as badges**: Posting core values while making decisions that contradict them. This is worse than having no values — it signals to the tribe that values are for employees while leaders are exempt.

- **Personality-cult Stage 4**: Your team's cohesion depends on your personal charisma rather than shared values and triadic structure. It will collapse the moment you leave.

- **The Pool of Shared Meaning is shallow**: Hard truths are not being said in your team. People agree in meetings and relitigate in hallways. Consensus is mistaken for alignment. You have a safety problem.

- **Managing failure magnitude instead of likelihood**: You have reduced the ambition of work to feel safer. You have proportionally reduced the achievable success. Restore the stakes; manage likelihood through preparation.

- **Underdeveloped bench**: You are the single point of failure on key decisions. Your team's ceiling is your personal bandwidth. You cannot scale.

- **The swoop-and-poop**: You drop into projects without prior context, make sweeping pronouncements, then disappear. You are setting expectations for yourself, not the team, and you are breeding resentment.
