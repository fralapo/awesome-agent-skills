# Patterns — Techniques a Creative Director Can Actually Do

Every concrete technique, method, and checklist pulled from the five role files, grouped by function. Each entry: *When* to use it, *How* to run it.

---

## Writing & Copy
*→ role: [copywriter.md](roles/copywriter.md)*

**Headline Approval Checklist** — *When:* before approving any headline. *How:* run seven checks: (1) specific benefit promised? (2) brand name included? (3) delivers news? (4) specific not vague? (5) flags right audience? (6) wrapped in quotes? (7) no period at the end? Fail any check → rewrite.

**Benefit Extraction** — *When:* first draft of body copy. *How:* separate attribute (what manufacturer put in) from benefit (what consumer gets). Crest has fluoride [attribute] → Crest gives your kids great checkups [benefit]. Copy must name the benefit, not the attribute.

**Single-Person Letter** — *When:* writing body copy. *How:* visualize one specific person receiving this as a personal letter. Write "you" in second person singular. Ask: would I send this to my neighbor? If not, strip formality and formula.

**Compressive Power Edit** — *When:* reviewing finished copy. *How:* mark every adjective; challenge each one to survive. Check for polysyllabic words; replace with monosyllables where possible. Count nouns and verbs — they should dominate. Read aloud; cut anything that slows the mouth.

**Story Structure for Copy** — *When:* structuring body copy. *How:* (1) open with consumer's problem or product news — no warm-up; (2) name the benefit; (3) prove with specifics — facts, numbers, demonstrations, testimonials; (4) write in story form; (5) include price if possible.

**Caption-First Rule** — *When:* approving print or digital layouts. *How:* check every image has a caption. Captions get 4× more readers than body copy. Caption must include brand name and benefit. No uncaptioned visuals.

**Killer Strategy Test (Lyons' Seven Questions)** — *When:* reviewing or writing a brief. *How:* ask: (1) single objective or cluttered? (2) focused or vague? (3) contains executional language? (4) language alive or stiff? (5) makes product unique or interchangeable? (6) names a real consumer concern? (7) confrontational and feisty? A strategy that fails any question is a placeholder, not a strategy.

**Starter Kit** — *When:* briefing a creative team. *How:* the CD or account lead writes up every creative thought about the assignment — consumer habits, cultural context, marketplace reality. This springboard earns trust and gives the team something real to push against.

**Radio Test** — *When:* evaluating whether a print or TV concept is idea-strong. *How:* reduce the concept to radio (voice only). If it doesn't work, the idea lives in production, not in thinking. Good radio writing makes its own pictures.

**Positioning Audit** — *When:* starting any new campaign. *How:* state in one sentence: what does the product do, who is it for? If the answer takes more than one sentence, the positioning is not set. Hold the answer for decades — never let novelty dislodge a working position.

## Generating Ideas
*→ role: [idea-generator.md](roles/idea-generator.md)*

**Binary Brief** — *When:* receiving any open brief before ideation. *How:* force three sequential binary decisions: (1) brand share or market growth? (2) existing users or triallists? (3) rational or emotional? Each produces a fundamentally different communication. Never generate against an open brief.

**Predatory Thinking Audit** — *When:* starting a competitive brief. *How:* map what every competitor is doing. Then map what no one is doing. The asymmetric angle competes on a different axis entirely. "I don't have to win. I just have to make you lose."

**Conflation (1+1=3)** — *When:* idea generation. *How:* write one element from the brief, then force-associate it with something from a completely unrelated domain. The third meaning that emerges between them is the concept. If no third thing appears, the pairing is wrong — try again.

**Five Entry Points Switch** — *When:* stuck on a brief. *How:* identify which entry point you've been using: (a) client name/identity, (b) business category, (c) medium itself, (d) message content, (e) audience's world. Switch to a different one. Stuck ideas almost always liberate when the entry point changes.

**Context Reframing** — *When:* a brief has a product weakness or constraint. *How:* don't defend against the weakness — replace the question. "Great place to live?" → "Great place to work?" Write three alternative questions the brief might secretly be better at answering.

**Recognition/Surprise Scoring** — *When:* evaluating ideas before presenting. *How:* score each idea on two axes 1–5: recognition (how familiar is the territory?) and surprise (how unexpected is the execution?). Kill anything with a low surprise score regardless of recognition. Only High/High goes to presentation.

**21-Type Wit Checklist** — *When:* adding wit to any concept. *How:* run through all 21 wit types and try at least 7 against the brief (Pairs, Ambiguity, Substitution, Addition, Modification, Missing Link, Homage, Trompe l'oeil, Puns, Taking it Literally, Expectations Confounded, Incongruity, Economy, Coincidence, Time Shift, Scale Shift, View Shift, Alphabet/Face, Transformations, Series of Twists, Bonus Wit). Force attempts before settling.

**Cue Testing** — *When:* finalizing a witty execution. *How:* show it to a proxy (not a colleague — someone who hasn't seen the brief). Ask: did they know a game was being played? How long did it take to get there? If they didn't search, the cue is too weak. If they found it instantly, there's no reward. Adjust cue strength accordingly.

**Reverse Journey Trace** — *When:* before committing to any witty idea. *How:* diagram your path A → B to the idea. Then trace B → A from the audience's position. Every step must be as obvious in reverse as it was obvious forward. If any step disappears, the wit won't land.

**Word Association Cascade** — *When:* unblocking. *How:* write every word associated with the brief for 10 minutes without editing. Let visual and conceptual associations cascade from unexpected entries. The productive idea is usually in the tail, not the head, of the list.

**"What If" Battery** — *When:* a concept is working but feels safe. *How:* invert the working assumption: what if all the loud parts were quiet? What if I removed every noun and kept only verbs? What if this were the first ad anyone ever saw? Extreme inversions expose the actual position on the spectrum so you can choose it consciously.

## Persuasion Levers
*→ role: [idea-generator.md](roles/idea-generator.md)*

**Anchor-First Sequencing** — *When:* presenting options, pricing, or campaign ideas. *How:* always lead with the highest-value, most ambitious option. Everything shown after will be judged against it, not against market averages. Never open with the safe middle option.

**Decoy Architecture** — *When:* designing product tiers or pricing. *How:* build three options; make the middle option the target; make the lower-end option clearly inferior to middle on one dimension (decoy). Never present a binary choice.

**Endowment Engineering** — *When:* designing trials, on-boarding, or product demos. *How:* enable genuine use (not just access) during the trial. Use "your" possessive language. Add personalization and configuration early. Make abandonment feel like a loss, not just a lapse.

**Zero Price Framing** — *When:* deciding whether to offer a free tier, free sample, or free trial. *How:* if FREE is used, design every subsequent touchpoint as a social-norm exchange (warmth, reciprocity, community), not a market-norm exchange (ROI, upsell). Introducing transactional language destroys the free-tier covenant.

**Norm Frame Audit** — *When:* reviewing any campaign, pricing page, or communications touchpoint. *How:* mark every signal as social-norm (warm, community, mission) or market-norm (price, ROI, efficiency). Mixed frames underperform both. Choose one frame and purge contradictions.

**Priming Sequence Design** — *When:* controlling what the audience reads or sees before encountering the work. *How:* the content before your content is part of your content. Brief the deck intro, the email subject line, the packaging unboxing, the store environment — all of it shapes actual experience. Sequence to protect, not contaminate.

**Premium Price Defense** — *When:* responding to price pressure or discount requests. *How:* remind the room that discounting a premium product degrades the product experience in the mind. Never apologize for being expensive; justify it with the experience the price creates.

## Leading & Feedback
*→ role: [creative-leader.md](roles/creative-leader.md)*

**Four Feedback Types** — *When:* choosing how to deliver a note. *How:* (1) Expectation-setting: before work begins — state what great looks like; (2) Task-specific: immediately after an action; (3) Behavioral: when a pattern repeats across tasks; (4) 360-degree: aggregated from multiple sources when your direct message is not landing. Match the type to the situation.

**STATE Delivery** — *When:* raising a sensitive issue or confronting underperformance. *How:* Share observable facts → Tell your story tentatively → Ask for their path genuinely → Talk tentatively → Encourage testing of your view. Never lead with a conclusion; lead with what you saw or heard.

**Contrasting Statement** — *When:* the other person goes defensive. *How:* construct a don't/do pair: "I'm not saying [misread of your intent] — I do want [actual message]." Deploy immediately when you see someone protecting their work rather than evaluating your feedback.

**Compliment Sandwich Elimination** — *When:* any feedback session. *How:* say the hard thing directly, with specific examples. Do not bracket it with praise. Top performers brace past positives and hear them as decoration.

**Verbal Confirmation Check** — *When:* closing any feedback conversation. *How:* ask "What are your takeaways and next steps?" Fight-or-flight during difficult feedback means a nod is not confirmation. The gap between what you said and what was heard is always larger than you expect.

**1:1 Ownership Transfer** — *When:* running weekly or bi-weekly 1:1s. *How:* have the report own the agenda. Your prompts: "What's most on your mind?", "Where are you stuck?", "What would make this week a success?" Dedicate one session per month exclusively to behavioral feedback and career — no project status.

**Would I Rehire Them? Test** — *When:* someone is struggling and you are uncertain about the next step. *How:* ask yourself: if this role were open today, would I hire this person again? If no, the move is already overdue.

**Purpose/People/Process Diagnostic** — *When:* a team is dysfunctional. *How:* ask in sequence: (1) unclear on goals? (Purpose.) (2) Wrong or unmotivated people? (People.) (3) Broken coordination? (Process.) Fix in that order. Do not skip to Process if Purpose is unclear.

**Stability + Challenge 2×2** *(Henry)* — *When:* diagnosing motivation/retention. *How:* plot the person on stability (clear expectations + protection) × challenge (risk permission + stretch). Low challenge → boredom/attrition; low stability → anger/burnout. Move the weak axis up. Leadership-side complement to the Fire Triangle.

**Fight for the Team** *(Henry)* — *When:* stakeholder pressure, scope creep, or a mid-project pivot lands. *How:* intercept upstream; have the hard conversation in private; return with a decision, not a debate. Never surprise the team with a reversal you didn't fight. The visible protection is what buys bigger creative risk.

**Maker→Manager Sweep** *(Henry)* — *When:* weekly, and whenever you feel the itch to "just do it myself." *How:* list where you slipped into execution; keep a few projects you stay hands-on; hand off the rest; for each rescue you did, ask whether coaching would reach the same outcome in two weeks instead of two hours.

**Autonomy-to-Competence Calibration** *(Henry)* — *When:* deciding how much latitude to give. *How:* match freedom to demonstrated skill + reliability; in 1:1s ask how much direction the person wants vs. gets, and adjust. Full freedom to someone who needs structure = setup to fail; micromanaging a senior = disrespect.

**Liked-vs-Effective Check** *(Henry)* — *When:* before hard feedback or an unpopular call. *How:* ask "am I doing this to be liked or to be effective?" If liked, recalibrate. Trust is earned by saying hard things clearly.

## Visual Craft
*→ role: [visionary.md](roles/visionary.md) (the "Visual Craft" half)*

**Leveling vs. Sharpening Diagnosis** *(Dondis)* — *When:* a layout feels off but you can't say why. *How:* read it against the felt axis (implied vertical + horizontal base) and the lower-left stress pull. Is it *leveling* (resolved/calm) or *sharpening* (tense/surprising)? Pick one — ambiguity between them reads as a mistake. Inert layout → push contrast, the most communicative pole of contrast/harmony.

**Type-as-System Spec** *(Spiekermann)* — *When:* setting any type. *How:* match typeface to the message's voice first; keep text type invisible and let display type carry hierarchy. Tune the six parameters together (size, leading, measure, tracking, word space, weight) — longer lines need more leading + looser tracking. Test at real output size, not on a 72-pt proof.

**Say-or-Show / Gutter Staging** *(McCloud)* — *When:* any word+image or sequential execution. *How:* say *or* show, rarely both — cut captions that repeat the image (duo-specific). Design what the reader completes *between* frames (closure); vary transitions; use space as time. Simplify what they should inhabit, render what they should observe (masking effect).

**Data-as-Design Pass** *(Tufte)* — *When:* the work carries charts/diagrams/data. *How:* maximize data density, minimize non-data ink, kill chartjunk. Use small multiples for comparison, layering/separation to beat the 1+1=3 clutter, and the smallest effective difference for every distinction. Treat the chart as a designed object, not an afterthought.

## Difficult Conversations
*→ role: [creative-leader.md](roles/creative-leader.md)*

**Start with Heart** — *When:* before entering any crucial conversation. *How:* clarify in writing or silently: What do I really want for myself? For the other person? For the relationship? Hold that goal as emotions spike. The moment your motive shifts from "resolve this" to "win," you have lost the conversation.

**Victim/Villain/Helpless Audit** — *When:* preparing for a hard conversation. *How:* audit your internal narrative. Are you editing out your own contribution (Victim)? Attributing worst possible motives (Villain)? Convincing yourself there is no option (Helpless)? Fix by asking: "What am I pretending not to know about my own role?" Tell the rest of the story before you speak.

**AMPP Listening Sequence** — *When:* someone has gone cold or escalated to hostility. *How:* Ask what's going on → Mirror emotion you observe → Paraphrase their story to confirm → Prime with your best guess to make it safe to confirm or correct. Silence and hostility are symptoms of fear, not malice.

**CRIB Deadlock-Breaker** — *When:* a creative review has become a position battle. *How:* Commit publicly to seeking mutual purpose → Recognize purpose behind their strategy (ask why, not what) → Invent a mutual purpose if goals are still incompatible → Brainstorm new strategies with shared purpose now established.

**Mutual Purpose/Respect Restore** — *When:* dialogue breaks down mid-conversation. *How:* step entirely out of the content. Name which condition is absent. Restore it with explicit language before re-entering the issue. Do not continue while either condition is missing.

## Culture
*→ role: [creative-leader.md](roles/creative-leader.md)*

**Stage Diagnosis by Language** — *When:* assessing team culture. *How:* listen to 5 minutes of real work conversation. Count "I" vs. "we." Map whether problems route to you (dyadic/Stage 3) or between peers (triadic/Stage 4). Stage 1 language: hostility and alienation. Stage 2: "we tried that." Stage 3: "I don't have enough time." Stage 4: "we" + values talk. Stage 5: wonderment.

**Triad Building** — *When:* operating at Stage 3 or beginning Stage 4. *How:* actively connect two other people to each other — not just to you. "Next time you go to Starbucks, take two friends, not one." Never build hub-and-spoke dyads. Measure: are problems solved peer-to-peer or routed through you?

**Core Values Elicitation** — *When:* trying to identify or codify team values. *How:* ask each team member to tell a story about how they learned one of their values. If they claim no values, ask what ticks them off — values appear in the second answer. Values must then be applied by leaders to real decisions first or they are posters.

**Oil Change** — *When:* quarterly, to maintain Stage 4. *How:* three questions in writing and then discussion: (1) What's working well? (2) What's not working well? (3) What can we do about what's not working? This is maintenance, not retrospective. Run it consistently; Stage 4 drifts back to Stage 3 without it.

**Noble Cause Declaration** — *When:* moving from Stage 4 toward Stage 5. *How:* craft one sentence stating the tribe's ultimate aspiration — so ambitious it is never checked off. Choose the adversary deliberately. The bigger the foe, the more powerful the tribal response.

**Fire Triangle Diagnosis** — *When:* creative energy drops or output goes flat. *How:* audit each leg before intervening. Flat ideas with low energy → check Oxygen (is there margin? freedom?). Compliance without investment → check Fuel (is purpose clear?). Quality declining, coasting → check Heat (are challenges big enough? is critique happening?). Apply the right lever; applying Heat to a team without Oxygen produces burnout.

**HALT Check** — *When:* important decisions during crunch periods. *How:* before proceeding, check: Hungry? Angry? Lonely? Tired? If any are yes — address it first. Institutionalize as cultural norm by giving explicit permission to step back.

## Taste & Vision
*→ role: [visionary.md](roles/visionary.md)*

**Input Diet Design** — *When:* building taste or resetting after a creative rut. *How:* audit what you consume weekly. Shift toward finest work across unrelated media — not to imitate but to raise the internal meter. Primary sources: nature, flea markets, vintage print, subcultural scenes, cross-disciplinary references. Trade press of your own field: deprioritize.

**Inspiration Box Maintenance** — *When:* always. *How:* maintain a physical, uncategorised archive of tear sheets, objects, fabrics, napkins. Do not organise or tag. Return at long intervals; the Sponge Process means inputs from 20 years ago may crystallize today.

**Eagle Eye Assignment** — *When:* starting a project. *How:* assign every sensory input — a meal, a cab, a conversation — to the current brief. Total immersion until daily life is raw material. Not metaphor; literal operational posture.

**Clean Table Reset** — *When:* starting any new project or season. *How:* explicitly forbid "what worked last time?" Strip labels from your own voice. List three assumptions about your aesthetic identity and challenge each one before opening the brief.

**Ecstatic Body Scan** — *When:* reviewing work. *How:* check for the Ecstatic response before intellectual assessment in every review session. Goosebumps, leaning forward, laughing in surprise — pre-rational, uncontaminated by social pressure. If nothing produces it anywhere, the work may not be there yet. Strong negative reactions also indicate live territory.

**Opposite Test** — *When:* any working assumption feels settled. *How:* try the exact opposite: more is less → try less; always color → try monochrome; sequential → try non-linear. The test is not to find a better way — it is to understand your actual position and choose it consciously.

**Conviction Test** — *When:* before release. *How:* ask three questions: Does this have conviction? Is this real? Is this accurate? Polish in the absence of conviction is noise. Refinement on top of conviction is craft. Do not release if you cannot answer yes to all three.

**Demo-itis Stop Rule** — *When:* obsessively reviewing finished or near-finished work. *How:* if you are not actively improving the work, stop consuming it. Repeated exposure calcifies a premature version as the real one. When you have extracted everything you know how to extract, release it.

## Creative Practice
*→ role: [visionary.md](roles/visionary.md)*

**Four-Phase Posture Check** — *When:* starting any work session. *How:* identify which phase you are in — Seed, Experimentation, Craft, or Completion — and adopt the posture for that phase only. Seed: collect without verdict. Experimentation: diverge without judgment. Craft: apply the Ecstatic compass. Completion: ask whether the work is ready to exist without you.

**Structure as Liberator** — *When:* sustaining a creative practice. *How:* fix when, where, and for how long you work. The decision not to decide every morning about your schedule frees creative energy inside the session. Enforce a deliberate reset ritual (Clean Table) before each project.

**Antenna Building** — *When:* daily. *How:* protect slow walks, pre-sleep reflection, mindful eating. Treat boredom and stillness as productive reception modes. Distinguish deliberate distraction (driving, showering — keeps conscious mind busy, frees subconscious) from procrastination (avoidance). Use the former; eliminate the latter.

**Big Idea Test Application** — *When:* before committing any campaign concept. *How:* run Ogilvy's five questions: Did it make me gasp? Do I wish I'd thought of it? Is it unique? Does it fit the strategy perfectly? Could it run for 30 years? All five must be yes. If not, return to ideation — do not proceed to production.

**Scary Goal Setting** — *When:* briefing a team or setting quarterly objectives. *How:* calibrate challenge to slightly exceed current skill — too far below = boredom; too far above = anxiety. Frame goals as imaginative aspirations, not effort requirements: "Dial up the worth, not the work." Ask the team to lean forward, not just work harder.

## Scoring & Refinement
*→ role: [evaluator.md](roles/evaluator.md)*

**Idea-Level Check (PASS 0)** — *When:* before scoring anything. *How:* place the idea on the Pollard 7-level taxonomy (business → brand → tagline → advertising → campaign → non_advertising → execution). Confirm it sits at the level the brief requires. Most common failure: an execution masquerading as a campaign ("let's make an AR filter"). Flag and re-level before evaluating.

**Weighted Scoring Run (PASS 1)** — *When:* deciding if an idea is good enough to present. *How:* score 1–10 on six criteria, multiply by weight, sum: Originality ×0.25, Strategic fit ×0.20, Emotional response ×0.20, Feasibility ×0.15, Scalability ×0.10, Simplicity ×0.10. Apply the saturation cap on Originality (3+ known campaigns with the mechanic → cap 7). Below 7 → do not present.

**Dual Calibration** — *When:* sanity-checking a score. *How:* run two independent scales — HumanKind (Cannes Gold 9.5+ … <7 redo) and Grey Scale (10 best in world … 1 toxic). If they diverge by more than 1.5 points, one is lying — re-evaluate.

**Gap Analysis** — *When:* score and HumanKind disagree. *How:* Score 8+/HK<7 = clever but doesn't matter → strengthen human purpose. Score<7/HK 8+ = matters but boring → strengthen craft/originality. Both 8+ = ship after scalability check. Both <7 = restart with new HMW.

**Multi-Perspective Panel** — *When:* a single score feels thin or contested. *How:* score the idea from four seats reusing the other roles as lenses — Copywriter (the line/benefit), Idea Generator (originality/bisociation), Creative Leader (would a real person pass it on?), Visionary (Cannes-jury bar). For the top 3 ideas, answer "Why isn't this a 9?"

**Method Rotation** — *When:* refining an idea across passes (PASS 2+). *How:* on each refine pass, generate with a *different* method from the one that produced the idea — same method reproduces the same blind spot. Rotation is mandatory; demand ≥3 methods from different categories upstream or originality is structurally capped.

**Kill Your Darlings** — *When:* you have a favorite. *How:* after picking it, argue *against* it. If the counter-argument is stronger than the idea, the idea is weak. Pair with the Pre-Mortem ("it's a year later and this flopped — why?") before exiting to articulation.
