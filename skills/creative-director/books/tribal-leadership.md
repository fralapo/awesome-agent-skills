# Tribal Leadership — Logan, King & Fischer-Wright
**One-line essence:** A diagnostic + upgrade system for creative directors to read team culture, identify which of five stages a tribe is stuck in, and apply the exact leverage points that move it forward.

---

## Core Frameworks

### The Five Tribal Stages

Culture is measured by the language people use, not by stated values or org-chart titles. Move the critical mass to the next stage and the tribe self-sustains. You cannot skip stages; you move people one at a time until a new dominant culture forms.

| Stage | % of Workplaces | Mantra | Mood | Relationship Structure |
|-------|----------------|--------|------|------------------------|
| **1** | ~2% | "Life sucks" | Despairing hostility | Alienated / gang clusters |
| **2** | 25% | "My life sucks" | Apathetic victims | Isolated individuals |
| **3** | 49% | "I'm great (and you're not)" | Lone warriors | Dyadic (star + support cast) |
| **4** | 22% | "We're great (and they're not)" | Tribal pride | Triadic networks |
| **5** | <2% | "Life is great" | Innocent wonderment | Ever-growing open network |

---

**Stage 1 — "Life sucks"**
- **Recognize:** Alienation, despairing hostility, gang-like loyalty, zero accountability, possible theft or violence. 2% of professionals; prisons and gangs are archetypal Stage 1.
- **How to upgrade:** Emphasize choice ("others' lives work — yours can too"). Encourage them to go where the action is: meetings, lunches, social functions. Cut ties with Stage 1 peers.
- **Success signal:** Shifts to "my life sucks" — now a victim, but no longer despairing. Looks like regression; it's progress.

**Stage 2 — "My life sucks"**
- **Recognize:** Passive-aggressive, minimum effort, crossed arms, "we tried that before." Mocks new initiatives. Protects people from management "intrusions." Coffee mugs: "I live for the weekends."
- **How to upgrade:** Build dyadic relationships one at a time. Show that their work matters. Assign short winnable projects. Connect them to late-Stage 3 mentors. Never team-build or motivate-speech a Stage 2 tribe — it fails completely.
- **Success signal:** Initiates dyadic relationships, shows personal accountability, moves from "we can't" to "I didn't."

**Stage 3 — "I'm great (and you're not)"**
- **Recognize:** Knowledge is power, hoarded. Lone warriors. Winning is personal. Two complaints: "I don't have enough time" and "no one is as competent as me." Dyadic-only networks. Typical of doctors, lawyers, salespeople, faculty, executives on their best days.
- **How to upgrade:** Assign projects bigger than one person can handle. Show that their success came from Stage 3 but the next level requires Stage 4. Describe Stage 4 role models. Coach toward transparency and information-sharing. Introduce triads. Tell stories about your own Stage 3→4 transition.
- **Success signal:** Shifts "I" to "we." Triads form. Time complaint disappears. Results increase ≥30%.

**Stage 4 — "We're great (and they're not)"**
- **Recognize:** Teams are the norm; values-based relationships; information flows freely; "we" language dominates. If two people squabble, a third steps in. Always has an adversary — "the bigger the foe, the more powerful the tribe." Tribal pride is the room's mood.
- **How to upgrade (stabilize):** Build triads, codify core values, define a noble cause, run tribal strategy sessions. Regular "oil changes" (what's working / not working / what to improve). Recruit to values. Don't solve problems yourself — point people to each other.
- **Success signal:** Language shifts to "life is great." Tribe self-polices; Stage 3 behavior is not tolerated. Leaders can upgrade most tribes one full level within 90 days.

**Stage 5 — "Life is great"**
- **Recognize:** No adversary needed; competing with what's possible, not other tribes. "Innocent wonderment." History-making bursts. First Mac team, Amgen at peak, 1980 US Olympic hockey.
- **How to upgrade/sustain:** Engineer market opportunities. Maintain Stage 4 infrastructure so when Stage 5 bursts end, the tribe returns to a stable Four. Stage 5 always recedes — it cannot be sustained indefinitely.
- **Success signal:** Results are making history. World is watching. Culture oscillates between 4 and 5.

### The Triad

The structural unit of Stage 4. A triad is a three-person relationship in which the Tribal Leader actively builds a connection *between two other people* — not just to each of them individually. Stage 3 leaders maintain dyads (hub-and-spoke); Stage 4 leaders build triads (mesh).

- **Use when:** Someone is stuck in Stage 3 hoarding behavior; or when you need to stabilize Stage 4. "Next time you go to Starbucks, take two friends, not one."
- **How:** Introduce two people with aligned values and mutual self-interest. State explicitly why they should know each other. Step back.
- **Why it works:** Power at Stage 4 comes from networks, not from knowledge. Triads scale trust; dyads bottle it.
- **Triad around Stage 3:** If your boss is Stage 3, build triads around them with the people you need — prove Stage 4 results, then invite the boss to join.

---

### Core Values + Noble Cause

The two anchors of a stable Stage 4 tribe. Without them, Stage 4 is a personality cult that collapses when the leader leaves.

- **Core values:** "A principle without which life wouldn't be worth living." Elicit by storytelling, not surveys. Ask "what ticks you off?" when people claim to have no values — values appear in the answer. Values must be lived by leaders first; posting them on badges while executives ignore them crashes culture to Stage 2.
- **Noble cause:** The tribe's ultimate aspiration — so ambitious it can never be "checked off." Amgen: "to serve patients." NTEU: "to ensure every federal employee is treated with dignity and respect." The noble cause creates alignment even when people don't fully agree.
- **Use when:** Stabilizing Stage 4. Values are the fuel; noble cause is the direction.

---

### Tribal Strategy

Stage 4 tribes build strategy from values outward, not market analysis inward.

Three test questions:
1. **Do the outcomes honor the values?** If no, wrong direction.
2. **Do the behaviors produce the outcomes?** If no, add behaviors leveraging available assets.
3. **Are we using all assets (people, relationships, knowledge, tools)?** Identify what's being underused.

Restrategize every 90 days. Focus only on behaviors (actions), not outcomes. Outcomes follow.

---

### The Tribal Leadership Epiphany (Stage 3→4 Transition)

The hardest jump. People don't move by being told — they have to see that Stage 3 will not get them where they want to go. The epiphany has three phases: intellectual (awareness that Stage 4 is different), emotional (feeling the hollowness of Stage 3 winning), spiritual/identity (no longer defining self by individual achievement). Only after all three does language genuinely shift.

**Trigger it by:** Pointing out that results require more than one person can deliver alone. Telling your own transition story. Describing what Stage 4 success feels like — not just looks like. Never force it; people who fake Stage 4 for career reasons collapse the culture when the leader leaves.

## Principles

- **Language first, behavior second:** Tribal Leadership only intervenes on observable words and relationship structures — never on beliefs, attitudes, or motivation. Change language → change relationships → change culture.
- **One stage at a time:** No shortcuts. A Stage 2 person cannot jump to Stage 4. Each stage must be visited.
- **The leverage points are stage-specific:** What works at Stage 2 (dyadic relationship-building) actively fails at Stage 3 (where it feels patronizing). Apply the wrong leverage and you reinforce mediocrity.
- **Critical mass, not unanimity:** Upgrade enough individuals and the tribe self-sustains the new stage — it then rejects people speaking the old language.
- **Bigger foe, stronger tribe:** Stage 4 tribes need an adversary. Choose it carefully: the more ambitious the opponent (an industry norm, a systemic problem), the more powerful the tribal response.
- **Values are not decoration:** The moment leaders make decisions that contradict stated values, culture drops — often all the way to Stage 2. Values must be applied to decisions, not just recited.
- **You can move forward only by bringing others:** Tribal Leaders cannot advance alone. Upgrading the tribe is how the leader earns recognition as the leader.

## Techniques

- **Dyad-to-Triad shift:** When someone is stuck in Stage 3, don't fight the dyadic structure — introduce a third person with shared values. Repeat until their network becomes a mesh rather than a hub.
- **Oil Change:** Regular team session with three questions: (1) What's working well? (2) What's not working well? (3) What can we do about (2)? Use quarterly. Keeps Stage 4 tribes from drifting back to Three.
- **Value story elicitation:** Ask team members to tell a story about how they learned one of their core values. Don't distribute a values survey. Stories create shared identity; surveys create compliance.
- **The "what ticks you off?" probe:** With Stage 2 groups that deny having values, ask what angers them — then ask why that matters. Core values appear in the second answer.
- **Stage-specific diagnosis:** Listen for dominant language in any conversation. Who uses "I" vs. "we"? Are relationships dyadic (hub-and-spoke) or triadic (mesh)? That tells you the stage — ignore the org chart.
- **Stage 3 around a Stage 3 boss:** Build triads with people around the Stage 3 leader. Prove Stage 4 results. Invite the boss to join rather than confront.
- **90-day culture upgrade:** Leaders can move a tribe one full stage in 90 days by consistently applying the stage-specific leverage points and building a critical mass of people at the next stage.

## Anti-patterns

- **Team building at Stage 2:** No amount of off-sites, motivational speeches, or values workshops changes a Stage 2 tribe. The Stage 2 language screen deflects all of it. Move to Stage 3 first.
- **Team building at Stage 3:** A group of "lone warriors" cannot be made into a team by declaring teamwork. They will perform Stage 4 while competing individually underneath. The epiphany must happen.
- **Naming someone's stage to their face:** Telling a Stage 3 person "you're Stage 3" triggers disproportionate anger and denial. Most Stage 3s believe they're at Stage 5. Use indirect signals — books, stories, role models.
- **Personality-cult Stage 4:** If Stage 4 is held in place by one leader's charisma rather than shared values and triadic structure, the tribe collapses to Stage 3 the moment that leader departs.
- **Values as badges:** Posting core values without living them as decision criteria is worse than having no values — it signals to the tribe that values are for employees while leaders are above them.
- **Solving problems yourself at Stage 4:** A Stage 4 leader who solves problems personally broadcasts "I'm great" — Stage 3 signal. Point people to each other; that's what builds the network.
- **Chasing Stage 5 as a permanent state:** Stage 5 always recedes. Organizations that chase it as an identity lose their Stage 4 infrastructure and crash hard. Treat Stage 5 as a burst, not a destination.
- **Two-stage self-bonus:** In research, people consistently rate themselves two stages higher than they are. Stage 3 people think they're Stage 5. Build diagnostic habits from observed language, not self-report.
