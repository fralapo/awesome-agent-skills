# Fired Up: Kindling and Keeping the Spark in Creative Teams — Andrew Johnston
**One-line essence:** The complete operating manual for sustaining creative output by managing environment, motivation, and heat — built on the Fire Triangle metaphor (Oxygen, Fuel, Heat).

---

## Core Frameworks

- **The Fire Triangle**: Three conditions must coexist for creative teams to burn: **Oxygen** (environment, margin, freedom), **Fuel** (freedom from fear, purpose, trust, fun, empowerment), and **Heat** (challenge, expectations, critique, direction, risk). Manage all three simultaneously. Fixing only one and ignoring the others extinguishes the fire. *When:* any time team energy or quality is flat. *How:* audit which leg is weak before intervening.

- **Good Juju / Bad Juju**: Team culture is the "hard-to-quantify mixture of mores and behaviors" that defines the vibe. Good Juju creates momentum and elevates everyone; Bad Juju creates inertia and makes people smaller. *When:* evaluating environment before adding process. *How:* assess whether your team's culture encourages connection, self-expression, and productive commotion — or suppresses them.

- **The In Between**: The ambiguous, unresolved state between Where You Are and Somewhere Else. Creatives are fired up by motion, surprise, and possibility — all qualities of the In Between. Leaders instinctively drive ambiguity out; this also drives creativity out. *When:* managing creative projects. *How:* hold expectations loosely, slow impulse to control, resist rushing to certainty.

- **Season of the Fire Hose**: Sustained overload where margin disappears, innovation collapses, and teams shift from exploration to survival. *When:* detecting burnout risk. *How:* add dry land (non-work investments), build levees (boundary commitments), and use the HALT check before key decisions.

- **HALT Check**: Before any important decision or commitment, team members check whether they are **Hungry, Angry, Lonely, or Tired**. If any condition is true, address it before proceeding. *When:* high-stakes moments during crunch. *How:* institutionalize as a cultural norm; give explicit permission to step back and refuel.

- **Flow Trigger (Csikszentmihalyi's Map)**: Flow — the optimal creative state — is entered only when challenge slightly exceeds current skill. Below the threshold: boredom and disengagement. Too far above: anxiety and shutdown. *When:* team is busy but uninspired. *How:* "Dial up the worth, not the work." Set Scary Goals that engage imagination, not just effort.

- **Doer → Leader Shift**: Great Doers focus directly on outcomes; great Leaders focus directly on people. The transition requires giving away three things: doing, answering questions, and controlling reputation. *When:* newly promoted leaders or leaders who keep rescuing the work. *How:* stop doing what needs to be done, stop answering questions (ask instead), risk more of your reputation on someone else's work.

- **Psychological Safety (Project Aristotle)**: Google's research found that team composition predicted performance less than team climate. The differentiator was **Psychological Safety** — confidence that you can expose real thoughts without being embarrassed or shut down. *When:* building or repairing a team. *How:* elevate collaboration over competition, cultivate other-centeredness, restructure work to require genuine interdependence.

- **Success/Failure Continuum**: Success and failure are not opposites; they exist at the **same end** of the continuum, with mediocrity at the other. They are inseparable (you can't have one without risking the other) and proportional (the size of possible success equals the size of tolerated failure). *When:* setting team ambition level. *How:* manage the **likelihood** of failure (precautions, preparation), never the **magnitude** (never reduce the stakes to feel safer).

- **Three Questions for Open Horizon**: To keep imagination alive against organizational pressure for productivity, pose: *"What could it be?"* (unleash prediction, suspend constraints), *"What would it be?"* (imagine future-state potential), *"What should it be?"* (connect to conviction and purpose). *When:* project kick-off or when team output feels derivative. *How:* sequence them in order; delay feasibility talk until after dreaming is complete.

- **30% / 90% Critique Rule**: Feedback must be calibrated to project completion stage. At 30%: broad directional thoughts, dream together, resist granular critique. At 90%: nit-pick everything, details are fair game. *When:* any feedback session. *How:* ask "where are we in the process?" before offering any note.

- **Clear Direction = Purpose + Particulars + Priorities**: Effective direction requires all three. Purpose anchors Why. Particulars specify the concrete What. Priorities force explicit trade-offs among competing demands. Absent any one, teams either drift or spin. *When:* kicking off work or resetting misaligned teams.

## Principles

- Creatives are fired up by **process** more than product — keep projects coming and limit downtime between them.
- Creativity is fundamentally an act of self-expression; environments that suppress individuality suppress imagination.
- "Functional capacity is less than finite capacity" — creatives are fully engaged before they are working at full capacity. Protect margin.
- Fear depletes; it never fuels. In sufficient quantity it produces learned helplessness: passivity, apathy, and inactivity become the rational response.
- Purpose is the fuel people run on. When Why is absent, What and How create a Tyranny of the To-Do List that exhausts without satisfying.
- The quality of what happens **between** team members matters more than the quality of the members themselves.
- "No Brilliant Jerks": competitive individual excellence that impairs team trust is net negative.
- Compromise is a method for consensus, not for quality. Protect creative decisions from political pressure that produces consensus at the expense of excellence.
- "Failure is not an option" is a leadership error. Saying it reduces tolerable magnitude of failure, which proportionally reduces achievable success.
- Top performers experience the absence of feedback as distress. Rationing critique toward poor performers causes top performers to leave and draws in slackers.
- Physical space is not decoration — it encodes what behavior is expected. Audit it as seriously as culture.
- Play is not frivolous: it drives innovation (suspends normal rules), collaboration (breaks professional barriers, builds authentic trust), and stamina (centers people in the present).
- Leaders model by going above and beyond **differently** than Doers — by attentiveness and support, not by doing the work themselves.

## Techniques

- **Atmospheric Engineering**: Design physical spaces and cultural rituals explicitly for connections, self-expression, and productive commotion. Designate collaboration space alongside individual work space. Treat "Couch Time" or informal gathering as a cultural asset to protect.

- **Consistent Surprise**: Prevent monotony (which squeezes oxygen from creatives) by being "consistently surprising" — not capricious, but deliberately varying the shape of projects, the structure of meetings, or the look of the room.

- **Make Additions for Volition**: When the Season of the Fire Hose makes leaders feel like victims, the counterintuitive move is to **add** to non-work life rather than manage work better. Invest in personal interests, reconnect with people you love, do household tasks before work. These restore the sense that you are choosing your life.

- **Build Levees**: Set explicit behavioral boundaries protecting personal priorities from work flood — "no shoptalk in bed," leave the phone in the car at the soccer game, hold morning coffee time sacred before opening work. Name the boundaries explicitly so others respect them.

- **30% Feedback Sessions**: Invite critique early and frame it as collaborative dreaming. Ask creatives to share at 30% completion; respond with broad direction and open questions, not execution notes. When teams learn you can calibrate feedback to the moment, they seek it earlier.

- **The Crit Pit**: Create a physical space dedicated to critique — bulletin boards, displays of current work, standing meetings. Making critique spatial and permanent demystifies it, makes it feel like mutual problem-solving rather than evaluation, and removes the stigma.

- **Scary Goals**: Set goals that engage imagination, not just effort. Present them as aspirational challenges worthy of top talent. The goal should make the team lean forward, not just work harder.

- **Match Challenge to Skill**: Continuously calibrate project assignments so the challenge slightly exceeds current skill level. Monitor for boredom (skill exceeds challenge) as aggressively as burnout (challenge exceeds skill by too much).

- **Other-Centered Perspective Prompt**: When teams need more creative solutions, ask "What would someone else do in this situation?" rather than "What would you do?" Polman & Emich research shows other-perspective produces 18% more creative solutions (66% vs 48% success rate on the same problem).

- **GEICO Project Labeling**: Name execution-only, low-creativity tasks explicitly (one team uses "GEICO Projects"). This right-sizes expectations and prevents creatives from over-investing. Batch these tasks between inspiring work and ensure no one has a steady diet of them.

- **Fail Forward Celebration**: Periodically name and celebrate significant failures as mile markers. This reframes failure culturally and signals that the team is aiming at the right end of the success/failure continuum.

- **Personnel Messaging Protocol**: When terminating or disciplining publicly visible team members, address what the situation is *not* without breaching confidentiality — "Decisions like this are never made quickly or easily and always involve sustained communication." This reduces fear contagion in the rest of the team.

## Anti-patterns

- **Driving Out Ambiguity**: Leaders who eliminate ambiguity to feel in control also eliminate the In Between — the source of motion, surprise, and possibility that fires up creatives. Resist rushing to certainty; hold expectations loosely.

- **Tightening Up Instead of Lightening Up**: When under pressure, defaulting to more process, more meetings, more oversight removes the oxygen creatives need. Often the right response is the opposite: less structure, more play.

- **Fear as Primary Motivator**: Fear produces short-term compliance and long-term learned helplessness. The team eventually lies down and stops. Desensitization requires escalating fear to produce the same effect — an accelerating death spiral.

- **The Doer in Leadership Clothing**: Leaders who continue doing the work, answering questions, and controlling outcomes steal their team's power, create dependency, and make team members feel incidental. "You steal people's power when you do too much yourself."

- **Premature Critique**: Offering granular, execution-level feedback early in the creative process shuts down imagination and trains teams to protect ideas from scrutiny by hiding them until late. Creatives stop sharing early and bring work only when it's too committed to change.

- **The Feedback Sandwich**: Bracketing negative feedback with positive comments appears helpful but reads as manipulative and disrespectful to creatives. It reduces positive feedback to a decorative preamble people learn to brace past.

- **Rationing Critique to Poor Performers**: Top performers experience feedback absence as abandonment and self-doubt. They leave. Their roles fill with less-invested people. Over time the team self-selects toward mediocrity.

- **Managing Failure Magnitude Instead of Likelihood**: Reducing the stakes of work to feel safer proportionally reduces the success available. Use precautions and preparation to manage likelihood; never change the ambition level to manage magnitude.

- **Pitchman Buy-In**: Minimizing the investment required to get compliance ("It won't take that long") produces action without commitment. When things get hard, compliance evaporates. Build commitment by pumping up purpose and never lowballing cost.

- **Superstar Team Assembly**: Assembling individual top performers does not produce team performance. Superstars are optimized for competition, which erodes the psychological safety that makes teams outperform individuals.

- **Premature Control**: Acting fast to resolve loose ends, ambiguities, or nascent conflicts removes the oxygen that creatives need. "Sometimes the choice not to act isn't lazy; it's leadership." Fast action addresses symptoms; slower responses let root causes surface.

- **No Drive-By Policy**: Eliminating spontaneous interaction in the name of efficiency recast unplanned connection as counter-culture, shutting down the informal trust-building that high-performance collaboration depends on.

## Signature Quotes

- "You can't force it to happen or control it directly. That kind of meddling stops the magic and spoils the transformation."
- "If you want creatives to connect when it counts, you need to encourage their connections when it doesn't."
- "Don't just do something, sit there."
- "Leaders looking for Flow need to dial up the worth, not the work."
- "If you want to fuel the fire in creatives, you often need to lighten up, not tighten up."
- "You've got to go big, or go home. There is simply no risk-free route to success."
