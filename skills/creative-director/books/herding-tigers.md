# Herding Tigers: Be the Leader That Creative People Need — Todd Henry

**One-line essence:** The definitive playbook for the maker-to-manager leap — how to give creative people the stability and challenge they need while fighting for them, setting clear direction, and earning trust without becoming a micromanager or a people-pleaser.

---

## Core Frameworks

- **Stability + Challenge (The Two Pillars)**: Creative people need two things above all else: a predictable container (stability) and work that stretches them (challenge). Stability = clarity of expectations + protection from organizational noise. Challenge = permission to take risks + work that pushes skills. — *How to apply:* Map your team against a 2×2 (high/low stability × high/low challenge). High stability + low challenge → boredom and attrition. High challenge + low stability → anger and burnout. Aim for both axes high.

- **Maker to Manager (The Identity Shift)**: You led by doing the work; now your job is to lead people who do it. These are fundamentally different games. Your creative instincts — which made you great as an individual contributor — will pull you back into execution mode, undermining your team's ownership. — *How to apply:* Weekly, do a mental sweep for moments when you slipped into maker mode. Reserve a handful of projects to stay directly involved; hand off everything else with intent.

- **Bounded Autonomy**: The sweet spot is freedom of action within clear boundaries — explicit expectations and principles that guide decisions, but room inside those boundaries for creative risk. Define the box precisely; then get out of the way. — *How to apply:* On each project, make explicit what is fixed (deadline, scope, core objective) and what is open (approach, execution, aesthetic choices). Communicate both, then trust the team to operate inside the open space without daily check-ins.

- **Fight For Your Team (Protection as Leadership)**: Your most underrated function is absorbing organizational pressure before it hits your team. Scope creep, last-minute pivots, stakeholder second-guessing — intercept these upstream. If your team sees you going to bat for them consistently, they will produce bolder, higher-risk work. If they see you passing pressure down or protecting your own reputation, trust collapses. — *How to apply:* When a stakeholder wants to change direction mid-project, have the conversation in private and return with a decision, not a debate. Never surprise your team with a reversal you didn't fight.

- **Control to Influence, Personal to Total Accountability**: The second shift, after maker to manager, is from controlling outcomes to shaping them through vision, principles, and coaching. You own the results of the whole team, not just the work you touched. — *How to apply:* Build a set of team principles that capture how and why decisions are made. Review them with the team regularly. When you would normally jump in and fix something, ask first whether coaching would get you to the same outcome in two weeks instead of two hours.

- **Liked vs. Trusted**: The need to be liked is one of the most common traps for creative leaders. It leads to softened feedback, avoided conflict, and watered-down direction — all of which hurt the work. Being trusted and respected is a better goal, and it requires saying hard things clearly. — *How to apply:* Before any difficult feedback or decision, ask: "Am I doing this to be liked or to be effective?" If the answer is the former, pause and recalibrate.

- **The Tiger Metaphor**: Talented creative people are powerful, not easily corralled, and a little unpredictable — like tigers. The job isn't to tame them (that destroys what makes them valuable) but to harness their energy toward a shared direction through trust, clarity, and strategic challenge. — *How to apply:* Treat each person as a unique entity requiring an individual strategy. What fuels one person may stifle another. The leader's job is to understand the individual and create the conditions for each person to operate at their peak.

- **Autonomy Calibrated to Competence**: Not every team member is ready for the same latitude. Match the degree of autonomy you extend to a person's demonstrated skill and reliability. Extending full freedom to someone who needs structure sets them up to fail; micromanaging a senior creative is its own form of disrespect. — *How to apply:* In 1:1s, explicitly discuss how much direction a person wants versus how much you currently give. Adjust iteratively.

## Principles

- Creative people need stability (clarity + protection) and challenge (permission + stretch) in equal measure — deprive them of either for long enough and the team withers.
- Your job as a creative leader is not to produce the best work in the room; it is to create the conditions in which your team produces work better than you could alone.
- Trust is the primary currency of creative teams. Spend it slowly, protect it fiercely, and never borrow against future trust for a short-term win.
- Fighting for your team is not optional — it is the most tangible signal of your leadership. "You need to fight for and defend your team." — Todd Henry
- Permission matters as much as direction. Creative people won't take real risks unless they believe you genuinely want them to.
- Don't try to be liked. As TV producer Jim Friedman put it: "It's not my job to be liked. It's my job to help them do their best work." The two sometimes coexist, but when they conflict, choose effectiveness.
- Giving someone a hard problem is an act of trust, not laziness. Holding people back from challenge is the quiet form of disrespect.
- Relational usury — promising future benefits you can't deliver to extract short-term effort — is a fast track to a team that stops believing in you.
- Vulnerability without credibility backfires. Establish your competence first, then invite people into your thought process.
- The transition from peer to manager is a social contract renegotiation, not just a title change. Have explicit conversations with former peers about what has changed.
- Great creative leaders balance ambition with humility: confidence in direction, openness to being wrong about execution.

## Techniques

- **Weekly priority sweep**: Each week, identify shifting project priorities and communicate them proactively to the team. Don't let people discover a change through a buried email.

- **One-on-one coaching conversations**: Schedule regular 1:1s focused on growth, not status updates. Ask: "How challenged by your work do you feel right now?" and "Do you need more clarity about anything we're doing?" Let them own the agenda.

- **Make decisions once, then move on**: Revisiting every call wastes the team's focus. When stakeholders want to re-open decisions, have that conversation in private, resolve it, and return with a final answer.

- **Reinforce stakeholder decisions with frequent touch points**: Don't let stakeholders drift and get cold feet. Remind them of the reasoning behind key choices throughout the project, not just at kickoff.

- **Explicit permission signals**: Verbally tell your team what risks you want them to take. If they don't hear it, they'll play it safe and look over their shoulders even when taking small risks.

- **Offer the first dumb idea**: In meetings where people are hesitant to share, volunteer an obviously imperfect idea yourself. It gives everyone else permission to speak without fear of looking bad.

- **Story library**: Collect and tell three types of stories — family/foundation (where you came from), motivations/milestones (what drives you), and mistakes/learnings (how you've grown). Stories build trust faster than policies do.

- **Protect focus time**: Intercept irrelevant information and scope-creep requests before they reach your team. "More information is not better. Better information is better."

- **Meeting hygiene**: Never chain back-to-back meetings across different projects. Always publish an agenda. Honor scheduled 1:1s — canceling them is a trust signal, not a scheduling issue.

- **Principles review**: Share your team's operating principles regularly and revisit *why* they exist. They exist to create accountability for how decisions are made, not to be a rulebook.

## Anti-patterns

- **Staying in maker mode**: Jumping in to fix the work instead of coaching the team through it. Gets you a good deliverable today and a dependent team forever.

- **Micromanaging out of ego**: The belief that your involvement improves every outcome. Often it signals distrust, kills ownership, and creates the very mediocrity it tries to prevent.

- **Selling out the team for self-protection**: Shifting blame downward or protecting your reputation at the expense of your team's. Destroys trust instantly and irreparably.

- **Softened feedback to preserve the relationship**: Diluting hard truths because you want to be liked. Serves neither the person nor the work. The team always senses the real evaluation anyway.

- **Scope creep passed downstream**: Absorbing every stakeholder request without filtering or negotiating. The team experiences the aggregate weight even when each request seems minor.

- **Treating 1:1s as status updates**: Using the highest-trust time you have with a report to discuss project progress instead of growth, challenge, and direction.

- **Superhero posturing**: Performing invulnerability to appear authoritative. The team sees through it, and it forecloses the authentic relationships that make hard moments navigable.

- **Peer-era habits with former peers**: Treating promotion as a social continuation of the old peer relationship. Clear, direct renegotiation of expectations is not awkward — it is required.
