# Creative Mischief — Dave Trott
**One-line essence:** A practitioner's toolkit for cutting through consensus thinking, winning attention, and making creative work that actually sells — drawn from advertising war stories, street philosophy, and deliberate rule-breaking.

---

## Core Frameworks

- **Desire & Permission**: Every purchase (and every persuasion) requires two elements: emotional desire (right brain) and rational justification (left brain). — *When:* Writing any brief or evaluating any creative work. *How:* Ask "Does this make them want it?" AND "Does this give them a reason they can tell themselves?" Both must be satisfied. A beautiful image creates desire; facts, stats, and social proof grant permission.

- **Predatory Thinking**: Don't aim to beat the best — aim to out-think, not out-muscle. — *When:* Competing for accounts, attention, or market share from a position of disadvantage. *How:* Study the environment, not just the opponent. Find the angle no one else is exploiting. "I don't have to win. I just have to make you lose." (Saatchi) / "I just have to outrun you." The goal is asymmetric advantage, not fair competition.

- **Context Reframing**: The same object, fact, or person changes meaning entirely when the frame changes. — *When:* A brief seems to trap you in a weak position or a product has no obvious advantage. *How:* Don't fight the existing frame — replace it. Docklands vs. Milton Keynes: switch the question from "great place to live?" to "great place to work?" Lazy behaviour became the solution, not the problem, when Singapore flipped the organ-donor default.

- **Binary Brief**: Strip every strategic decision to two options; force a choice at each stage before writing anything. — *When:* Briefs are vague, multi-objective, or too long. *How:* Three sequential binary questions: (1) Brand share or market growth? (2) Triallists or current users? (3) Product (rational) or brand (emotional)? Each choice produces a fundamentally different communication. Never try to do two things at once.

- **Rock Logic vs. Water Logic**: Rock resists and holds position; water flows around obstacles and finds unexpected paths. — *When:* Facing a constraint, a bad brief, a difficult client, or a blocked career. *How:* Don't fight the obstacle head-on. Ask what you CAN do instead of lamenting what you can't. Follow your energy; explore adjacent territories. The water always gets through.

- **Conflation (1+1=3)**: Put two unexpected, unrelated things together to produce a third effect that neither contains alone. — *When:* Creating concepts, headlines, or film ideas that need to feel surprising. *How:* Reject linear narrative. Use Eisenstein's editing principle: juxtapose disparate images/ideas to generate mood, meaning, or emotion that transcends both. The whole must exceed the sum.

- **Objective vs. Subjective critique**: Professionals evaluate work on function, not taste. — *When:* Reviewing, presenting, or defending creative work in any context. *How:* Replace "I like it / I don't like it" with "It works because…" / "It doesn't work because…". The three-part construction forces reasoning: it (the work, not you), works (function, not feeling), because (evidence required). Subjectivity is for consumers; objectivity is for practitioners.

- **Wu Wei (Action by Inaction)**: The best result is sometimes achieved by doing as little as possible. — *When:* Negotiations, pitches, internal politics, and new-business situations where forcing the issue would backfire. *How:* Identify the structural forces already working in your favour and let them run. The client who delays long enough will have no time for a competitive pitch. Silence in negotiation transfers power to the person who can wait longest.

## Principles

- **Interesting beats right.** Most advertising fails not because it's wrong but because it's been processed into safety. Being right is overrated — being interesting is what gets noticed, remembered, and acted on. "Never let the truth spoil a good story." (Churchill)
- **The brief is a floor, not a ceiling.** Read and sign off the brief, then forget it when you look at the work. Consumers will never read the brief before encountering the ad — don't judge it as if they had.
- **Form can be emotional function.** Aesthetics, beauty, and style are not decoration — they can be the functional core of the work. The lipstick in the concentration camp restored humanity. Never dismiss the "merely beautiful."
- **Sound is more powerful than vision.** The soundtrack shapes what the eye believes it sees. The thing audiences carry home and pass into language is almost always sonic, not visual. Build sound into creative work from the start.
- **Simplicity is genius.** "The mind is not a vessel to be filled, but a fire to be ignited." (Democritus) Complexity is a hiding place, not a strategy. Go beyond complicated to get to simple; simple is where power lives.
- **Don't let anyone else write your agenda.** When opponents provoke you into playing their game on their terms, they've already won. Stay in your own game; "Act, don't react." (Buddha)
- **Outrageous energy, directed.** Rebelliousness without purpose is vandalism. Apply it to the work. The best creative people find a direction for disruptive energy; they don't suppress it.
- **Prove yourself through volume of work, not arguments about credit.** The weight of credibility comes from doing great work repeatedly with different people on different accounts. Don't get stuck carrying the woman — leave her at the river.
- **Opportunities don't appear; you create them.** Waiting for the right moment, the right break, the right brief is putting the problem in someone else's hands. If no one makes it happen, it doesn't happen.
- **Reasonable people don't do much.** The breakthroughs — in sailing, in advertising, in life — come from people who ignore other people's definitions of reasonable. Do what makes sense to you, then do it.
- **The punters haven't read the brief.** Look at work fresh, as a consumer would. Ask: does it stand out? Did I enjoy it? Do I know who it's for? Do I know what it's saying? Only then check the brief.
- **Don't trust anyone to do your thinking.** Question the brief. Question the category rules. Jack Charlton re-read the eligibility rules and suddenly had twice as many players. Always behave like the underdog.
- **A principle isn't a principle until it costs you money.** (Bernbach) Until you've actually refused business or killed work because of what you believe, you don't actually know what you believe.

## Techniques

- **Upside-down ad review**: Look at layouts rotated 180° before you can read the words. Your right brain (visual) activates and you see the graphic quality honestly. Use to audit design strength independent of copy.
- **Cross-someone-else-to-present-it**: When critiquing student (or junior) work, have someone other than the creator present it. If the idea isn't clear to the presenter, it won't be clear to the public. Immediate, unarguable proof that the ad doesn't communicate.
- **Half-hour strategy sprint**: Give teams a random product and 30 minutes to produce a strategy and campaign thought. Forces intuitive strategic thinking; makes visible who can think fast and who can only think safe.
- **Horror movie sound test**: Turn the sound off a scary scene — far less frightening. Apply to commercials: if stripping the audio destroys the effect, you're relying on sound you haven't consciously designed. Design it deliberately.
- **Pascal's Wager preparedness**: Always do the small, cheap things that give you an asymmetric advantage in low-probability but high-consequence situations (memorise the exits; research the person who won't come in). There are always good reasons not to — ignore them.
- **Binary brief stripping**: Take any long brief and run it through three binary questions (brand share vs. market growth / triallists vs. users / product vs. brand) to isolate the single communication job. Refuse to write until one answer is chosen at each stage.
- **Teach to train yourself**: Running student crits is crash-course training in being a creative director. Fast analysis of 20 campaigns in two hours forces clear, simple, fast thinking. Use it systematically to develop your department.

## Anti-patterns

- **"I like it / I don't like it"**: Subjective opinion stated as a verdict. Fails on three counts: centres the speaker instead of the work, reveals feeling not thought, and is impossible to debate rationally. Replace with "It works because…"
- **The correct brief, presented first**: Briefing everyone on the strategy before showing them creative work trains them to evaluate compliance, not effectiveness. It ensures the work is judged in the one way consumers will never use.
- **USP abused as any difference**: The Unique Selling Proposition only works when the difference is both genuinely unique AND something people actually want. A marginal improvement nobody cares about, dressed up as a USP, produces factually accurate but completely inert advertising.
- **Overproduction as substitute for thinking**: Spending the most on a commercial, bragging about its budget — this is the opposite of the Enlightenment lesson. It's better to out-think than out-spend. Trompe l'oeil beats marble every time.
- **Living in your own head**: Judging finished work against the version in your head is the wrong comparison. The public never saw what was in your head. You're the only one who knows what's in there — and you might be wrong.
- **Scepticism confused with cynicism**: Scepticism — "I won't believe it until you prove it" — is the source of all knowledge. Cynicism — "I won't believe it even if you prove it" — is the source of ignorance. Cynics in creative departments are expensive.
- **Playing your opponent's game**: Any time you're drawn into internal politics, awards-chasing, or credit disputes on someone else's terms, you've conceded the match. They must be better at their game than you are — why play?
- **Research as thumbs up/down**: When research becomes the decision-maker rather than one source of input among several, it rewards the least surprising, least attention-getting work. Broad humour that qualitative sessions might kill can be exactly what makes a campaign famous.

## Key Topics

| Topic | Big idea |
|---|---|
| Attention | Visibility is not about being right — it's about being interesting. Extremes get noticed; the processed middle disappears. |
| Briefing | The binary brief forces one honest strategic decision at each stage before any creative work starts. |
| Clients | The account man's job is to get the client to want what they need — not to do what the client wants, and not to refuse. |
| Competitive strategy | Predatory thinking means finding asymmetric advantages, not fighting fair. Study what others aren't noticing. |
| Context | Reframe the question, not the answer. What looks like a weakness in one frame is strength in another. |
| Creative energy | Rebelliousness is a resource. Directed, it produces great work; undirected, it's just trouble. |
| Creative people | Really creative people are novelty-hungry, perpetually stimulated by new things — not just new technology. They discover, they don't wait. |
| Decision-making | Our minds don't interpret reality — they create it. Choose the reality that empowers you and live it. |
| Ideas | Real life is the richest source. The skill is in spotting it, not inventing it from scratch. |
| Mind / Gestalt | The mind is a pattern-making machine that defaults to lazy groupings. You must consciously fight its tendency toward easy categorisation. |
| Pitching | A creative pitch is not the same as a pitch with creative work. Theatre, demonstration, and conviction can win accounts that polished decks cannot. |
| Positioning | A brand needs one point of difference. Being one of forty-four is forgettable; being one of one is a brand. |
| Sound | Sound determines what the eye believes it sees. Design it intentionally — it outlasts visuals in cultural memory. |
| Teaching | Training others is how you train yourself to be a creative director. Set the game up so everyone wins. |
| Brand vs. product | People buy products for what they do; they buy brands for what they say about them. The badge is often the real product. |
