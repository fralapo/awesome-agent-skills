# The Making of a Manager — Julie Zhuo
**One-line essence:** A practitioner's playbook for new and growing creative leaders — how to give feedback that sticks, run 1:1s that matter, hire with intent, design meetings that aren't theater, and scale yourself without losing grip on quality.

---

## Core Frameworks

- **Purpose / People / Process (The Three Buckets)**: Every management task sorts into why the work matters, who is doing it, and how they work together. — *When:* diagnosing any team dysfunction or deciding where to invest time. *How:* Ask sequentially — is the team unclear on goals (purpose)? Wrong/unmotivated people (people)? Broken coordination (process)?

- **Output over Activity**: Judge yourself and your reports on outcomes, not busyness. "Measure a salesman by the orders he gets, not by the calls he makes" (Grove). — *When:* evaluating performance, reviewing your own week. *How:* For each area you own, name the measurable result. Strip activity metrics from your status updates.

- **The Two-Axis Evaluation (Chris Cox Framework)**: Rate a manager on (1) team results and (2) team strength and satisfaction. Present outcomes + future capability. — *When:* quarterly self-assessment, reviewing managers under you. *How:* Score both axes independently; a green-results / red-team score is a ticking clock.

- **Four Types of Feedback**: (1) Set clear expectations before work begins. (2) Task-specific feedback immediately after an action. (3) Behavioral feedback connecting patterns across tasks. (4) 360-degree feedback aggregated from multiple sources. — *When:* choose the type based on whether the situation is about a single output, a recurring pattern, or a complete performance picture. *How:* Default to task-specific first; escalate to behavioral when you see repeated themes; run 360s at review cycles or when your message isn't landing.

- **The Four Entry Paths**: Apprentice (promoted from within), Pioneer (founding a new group), New Boss (hired in from outside), Successor (replacing departing manager). — *When:* onboarding yourself or a new manager. *How:* Each has distinct pitfalls — Apprentice must renegotiate peer relationships; Pioneer must codify tacit knowledge; New Boss must listen before acting; Successor must avoid over-inheriting the past.

- **The Extended Vacation Test (Great Bench)**: If you disappeared for three months, how much would your own manager need to step in? — *When:* assessing team health and delegation depth. *How:* If the answer is "a lot," your next layer of leadership needs developing before you can scale further.

- **The Delegation Tightrope**: Spend time at the intersection of (1) most important to the organization and (2) what you uniquely do better than anyone else. Delegate everything else. — *When:* deciding whether to solve a problem yourself or hand it off. *How:* If a report can handle it at 80%+ quality, coach and hand off. Reserve yourself for vision communication, leadership hiring, and cross-group conflict resolution.

- **The Recruiting Funnel as Math**: Hiring is a numbers game. Define your conversion rates (email → interest → interview → offer → accept), then treat each step as an operational metric to optimize. — *When:* scaling a team past 5+ open roles. *How:* Break the annual hire target into weekly email and interview volume; have each manager own a slice.

## Principles

- The job of a manager is to get better outcomes from a group of people working together — not to be the best individual contributor on the team.
- Leadership must be earned; management is a role that can be given. You may have the title before you have the trust.
- Every major disappointment is a failure to set expectations — trace back any missed landing to where the contract was unclear.
- Feedback only counts if it changes behavior. Delivering it doesn't close the loop; watching what changes does.
- Positive feedback is your most underused lever — at least 50% of your feedback should be reinforcing what's working.
- Hire for where the team is going, not where it is. Always hire people capable of more than the role currently requires.
- Diversity in background, experience, and perspective produces better creative outcomes — seek it actively, not as a slogan.
- Reject "weak hires" (unanimous-but-unenthusiastic). Wait for a passionate advocate. Every hire is a gamble; only take bets someone believes in.
- Giving a report a hard problem is a sign of trust, not laziness — talented people want to be challenged.
- The goal of management is to make yourself replaceable in your current job so you can grow into the next one.
- Great managers are made, not born — the only prerequisite is caring enough to try and enjoying the people side of the work.

## Techniques

- **Monthly behavioral 1:1**: Dedicate one 1:1 per month exclusively to behavioral feedback and career trajectory — no project status, no task updates.

- **Structured 1:1 agenda**: Have reports own the agenda; your job is to listen and probe, not to deliver updates. Useful prompts: "What's most on your mind?", "Where are you stuck?", "What would make this week a success?"

- **Expectation-setting contract**: At the start of any significant project or new role, explicitly state what great looks like, what mediocre looks like, what common pitfalls to avoid. Revisit and recalibrate proactively, not reactively.

- **Feedback template for critical conversations**: "When I [heard/observed/reflected on] your [action/behavior/output], I felt concerned because… I'd like to understand your perspective and talk about how we can resolve this." — Direct, specific, non-personal, collaborative close.

- **Verbal confirmation close**: End any important feedback conversation with "What are your takeaways and next steps?" to surface whether the message landed as intended.

- **360 email cadence**: Every quarter, email 4–5 of each report's closest collaborators: (a) What is X doing especially well that X should do more of? (b) What should X change or stop doing?

- **Annual org planning**: Each January, map a future org chart. Identify skill gaps, diversity gaps, and open roles needed. Having the plan prevents panic-hiring when someone departs.

- **Preferred interview questions (Zhuo's five)**: (1) What kinds of challenges interest you and why? (2) What are your greatest strengths / what would peers say are your growth areas? (3) Where do you hope to be in three years? (4) What was your hardest conflict in the past year and how did it end? (5) What's inspired you in your work recently?

- **"Would I rehire them?" test**: When a manager or contributor is struggling, ask yourself: "If the role were open today, would I hire this person again?" If no, the move is overdue.

- **Meeting type pre-assignment**: Before scheduling any meeting, name which of five purposes it serves — decision, information-share, feedback/review, idea generation, or relationship-building. Design the meeting for that purpose only.

- **Delegation rule of thumb**: Anything a report can do as well or better than you → delegate immediately. Anything only you can do that is critical to the org → protect jealously.

## Anti-patterns

- **The Compliment Sandwich**: Padding critical feedback with praise before and after dilutes the message and reads as insincere. Say the hard thing directly, with specific examples, then invite dialogue.

- **Consensus-seeking on decisions**: Managing through consensus avoids short-term conflict but produces slow, watered-down outcomes. Gather input broadly, decide clearly, own the call. "Disagree and commit" is a healthy team behavior.

- **Status updates in 1:1s**: Using rare 1:1 face-time for information transfer wastes its highest-value use. Async tools exist for status; 1:1s are for coaching, development, and trust.

- **Weak hires**: Accepting a unanimous-but-unenthusiastic "hire" vote because no one can find a reason to say no. Weak hires rarely add meaningful impact; they consume onboarding energy and raise the average mediocrity of the team.

- **The Swoop and Poop**: Dropping into a project without prior context, making sweeping pronouncements, then disappearing. Sets expectations for the manager, not the team, and breeds resentment. Announce your involvement cadence upfront.

- **Holding onto IC work too long**: Every apprentice manager clings to individual contributor output past the point where it's sustainable. When your team reaches 4–5 people, you need a plan to reduce your IC load — not add to it.

- **Framing decisions as questions when they're already made**: Asking "What do you think?" when your mind is already set creates false participation and makes you look disingenuous when you don't change course.

- **Assuming your message landed**: The gap between what you said and what was heard is always larger than you expect. Fight-or-flight kicks in for the recipient during critical feedback; a nod is not confirmation.

- **Individual contributor as manager**: Spending your management time as the best designer/writer/strategist on the team is an additive contribution, not a multiplicative one. The multiplier effect only kicks in when you develop and deploy others.

- **Underdeveloped bench**: Running a team where you are the single point of failure on key decisions. When you can't be removed without chaos, you've constrained the team's ceiling to your personal bandwidth.
