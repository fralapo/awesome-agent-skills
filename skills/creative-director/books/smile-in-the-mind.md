# A Smile in the Mind: Witty Thinking in Graphic Design — McAlhone & Stuart
**One-line essence:** The definitive taxonomy of visual wit — how to generate, evaluate, and deploy ideas that make meaning happen inside the audience's mind, not on the surface of the design.

---

## Core Frameworks

- **Bisociation** (Koestler): All wit requires exactly two elements colliding. *When:* you have a single message and no idea yet — locate the second element first. *How:* find what the brief already contains (client name, medium, audience, message) and ask what else it secretly is; the collision point is where the wit lives.

- **The Familiar + The Play**: Wit is always structural — take something the audience already knows and twist it. The familiar provides recognition; the play produces surprise. *When:* assessing your own idea. *How:* score the idea on both axes. Low recognition + low surprise = failure. High recognition + low surprise = obvious. Low recognition + high surprise = baffling. High on both = a hit. Use this matrix as a go/no-go filter before presenting.

- **The Recognition/Surprise Matrix**:

  | Recognition | Surprise | Result |
  |---|---|---|
  | Low | Low | Total failure |
  | High | Low | Obvious, weak |
  | Low | High | Baffling, alienating |
  | High | High | Hit — witty |

- **The Clothesline Model**: A straight communication travels 100% from sender to receiver; the audience contributes nothing. Wit never travels the full distance — the designer goes 60–95%, leaving a gap the audience must cross. *When:* calibrating how much to reveal. *How:* ensure the cue is strong enough that crossing the gap feels satisfying, not frustrating. The audience completing the idea IS the communication.

- **The Cue**: The single most important craft variable. The better the cue, the larger the leap the audience can make and still land safely. Koestler: "Words are merely stepping stones for thought. Economy means spacing them at intervals just wide enough to require a significant effort." *When:* reviewing a draft idea. *How:* test the cue on a proxy audience. A witty idea needs to signal that a game is being played — otherwise arbitrary design trains audiences to stop looking for meaning.

- **Bonus Wit**: Wit embedded subtly so the piece works without it — bonus for those who find it. *When:* audience is broad or skeptical; when a client won't tolerate overt cleverness. *How:* design the piece to communicate plainly; layer the wit so it's a reward, never a requirement.

- **Wit as Chord vs. Melody**: Single-image wit (poster, logo, pack) = a chord — one total statement, one culmination. Sustained wit (calendar, campaign, literature) = a melody — series of minor explosions across time. *When:* choosing how much to load onto one moment versus stretching across a sequence. *How:* series formats demand a central twist plus variations; single formats demand everything to resolve in one beat.

- **Five Entry Points for Ideas**: Root the idea in (a) client, (b) client's business, (c) medium, (d) message, or (e) audience. *When:* stuck. *How:* switch entry point systematically; a stuck idea usually comes from staying at one point too long.

- **The Reverse Journey**: The audience decodes in the opposite direction from the designer. If you reached the idea via A → B, the audience must navigate B → A. *When:* checking that an idea will communicate. *How:* trace backward from your visual to its intended meaning; every step back must be as obvious as the corresponding step out.

## Principles

- An idea that happens in the mind stays in the mind. Depth of encoding determines durability of recall — wit burns the idea in via the pleasure of decoding.
- The click of pleasure is a brain event: recognition + surprise triggers a reward response. Design that short-circuits this (over-explains) removes the reward and loses the memory trace.
- Wit implies joining: it says "if you get this, you're one of us." Use when you want to create allegiance, not just attention.
- Context, surprise, and actuality (the X factor) multiply the impact of wit in the wild — the same idea in a book is always weaker than the same idea encountered unexpectedly in its intended setting.
- Wit is structurally identical across verbal and visual domains; the techniques are the same, only the medium differs.
- Real wit lasts. Work that was groundbreaking loses its edge when copied, but the best ideas survive because the structure is irreducible.
- Too clever is dumb (Ogden Nash). Designer ego produces over-coded ideas that alienate. Wit's purpose is communication, not display.
- "How familiar can it be so that it's understood, and yet how unusual can it be so that it is engaging — that interval is a critical interval in design." (Milton Glaser)

## Techniques

**The 21 Types of Wit** (the full taxonomy — use as a generative checklist at the start of any brief):

- **Pairs**: Juxtapose two things to reveal unexpected similarity or significant difference.
- **Ambiguity**: Create an image readable two ways simultaneously — both readings valid.
- **Substitution**: Replace one element of A with B; B sits in A's context like a cuckoo in the nest.
- **Addition**: Add something to A that transforms it into B.
- **Modification**: Make A take a form that calls B to mind.
- **Missing Link**: Show B; let the audience deduce A. "See this, think that."
- **Homage**: Dress A in the visual language normally associated with B.
- **Trompe l'oeil**: Real fighting fake — the illusion IS the idea.
- **Puns and Rebuses**: Visual/verbal equivalences where syllables or images translate across registers.
- **Taking it Literally**: Render a metaphor or figure of speech as a literal image.
- **Expectations Confounded**: Set up a pattern, then violate it at the payoff moment.
- **Incongruity**: Clash elements that jar — two-ness through incompatibility.
- **Economy**: One element plays two roles — double duty, double payoff.
- **Coincidence**: Exploit genuine structural similarity between two otherwise unrelated things.
- **Shift: Time**: Displace the expected moment — the past/future intruding into the present.
- **Shift: Scale**: Change scale radically to reframe meaning.
- **Shift: View**: Change viewpoint or perspective to reveal a hidden truth.
- **Alphabet / The Face**: Use letter forms or facial configurations as the formal substrate for a second meaning.
- **Transformations**: A design that starts as one thing and becomes another over time.
- **Series of Twists**: Theme and variations; the sustained campaign or calendar format.

**Generative methods from practitioners:**
- **Word lists**: Write every word associated with the brief; let visual associations cascade from unexpected entries.
- **Six briefs at once** (John McConnell): Work multiple problems simultaneously — peripheral attention on other briefs frees the mind from tunnel lock on the current one.
- **Oblique approach** (Gide): Work on the problem next to the problem; approach at an angle.
- **Off moments**: Actively protect unfocused time — commutes, walks, half-sleep. Ideas crystallize when the mind is untethered.
- **Automatic visual scratching** (Alan Fletcher): Sketch without goal; let the hand produce something the eye misreads productively.
- **Deliberate mis-seeing**: Remove glasses, step back, blur — ambiguity in the visual field triggers unexpected connections.
- **The Joker system** (The Partners): When stuck, buy 30 minutes of fresh-eye input from someone on a different project. Rules-based cross-pollination, not informal chat.
- **Release the wilful demand** (Glaser): Ideas arrive when you stop forcing them. Create the conditions; don't try to manufacture the moment.

## Anti-patterns

- **Big-head design**: Inserting the difficulty of the creative process into the solution to signal intelligence. Wit and ego don't mix; self-indulgence produces work that excludes rather than includes.
- **Over-coding**: Too many veils between the idea and its decoding. The audience senses a padlock but cannot find the key; engagement tips into frustration and rejection.
- **Under-cueing**: A witty idea with no signal that a game is being played. Arbitrary design has trained audiences to ignore things that look "odd" — they need a clear indicator that a meaningful puzzle exists.
- **Familiarity without surprise**: Ideas that are too on-the-nose — following the instructions of a rebus, or adding a tear to an existing logo. The gap is too small; no reward, no memory.
- **Surprise without familiarity**: Ideas so far from shared reference that they baffle. The designer has entered "the abstruse reaches of the far-fetched"; the solution is unfathomable. In communication, unfathomable equals unusable.
- **Controlling idea mistaken for witty idea**: A controlling idea governs all decisions on a piece (tone, structure, content) but is not itself playful. A witty idea adds frisson — it is more than just intelligent. Confusing the two produces strategy without spark.
- **Ignoring the reverse journey**: Designing the outward path (designer to idea) without testing the inward path (audience back to meaning). Associations that are obvious going out can be ambiguous or wrong coming back.
- **Context gap**: Showing wit without the context the audience needs to decode it. Shared domain (cultural, temporal, geographic) must exist before wit can land.

## Key Topics

| Topic | Big idea |
|---|---|
| What wit is | Structural play on the familiar; always two elements; recognized and decoded in the mind, not on the surface |
| What wit does | Wins time, invites participation, gives the pleasure of decoding, rewards, amuses, gets under the guard, forms a bond, goes deeper, is memorable |
| The cue | The craft lever that determines whether wit communicates or alienates; spacing the stepping stones "just wide enough" |
| Bisociation | The two-element collision underlying all wit, verbal or visual; Koestler's term adopted as the book's master concept |
| 21 types | A generative taxonomy — seven structural groups covering comparisons, two-in-one hybrids, shifts, deductive moves, time-based changes, double-duty elements, and coincidence |
| Danger / misfires | Over-coding, under-cueing, ego-display, context gaps, and reverse-journey failures — the five ways wit kills communication |
| How ideas arrive | Off moments, the fertile borderland of half-sleep, multi-job parallel working, oblique approaches, word lists, deliberate mis-seeing, and structured cross-team input |
| Bonus wit | Hidden ideas that reward discoverers without penalizing those who miss them — the safest deployment in uncertain contexts |
| Wit at first hand | The X factor (surprise + context + actuality) multiplies impact in the real world vs. a book; never evaluate work only in reproduction |
