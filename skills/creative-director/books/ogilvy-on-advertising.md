# Ogilvy on Advertising — David Ogilvy
**One-line essence:** A practitioner's handbook for making advertising that actually sells — not awards bait — built on decades of research-backed principles for headlines, copy, TV, brand, and agency leadership.

---

## Core Frameworks

- **Positioning**: "What the product does and who it is for." — *When:* Before any creative work begins. *How:* Choose one clear positioning and commit to it for years. Dove = toilet bar for women with dry skin (not a detergent). SAAB = car for Norwegian winters. VW Beetle = protest against Detroit vulgarity. A position held for 25 years compounds. Never let a new marketing director reposition for novelty.

- **Brand Image**: "Products, like people, have personalities." — *When:* Every single ad is a deposit or a withdrawal. *How:* Every ad should be a consistent contribution to one personality. Jack Daniel's = homespun honesty. Marlboro = cowboy freedom. You're not selling the whiskey — you're selling the image; "they are tasting images." Cheap-looking advertising makes people feel cheap for owning the product.

- **The Big Idea Test** (5 questions): *When:* Evaluating any creative concept before committing. *How:* Ask all five:
  1. Did it make me gasp when I first saw it?
  2. Do I wish I had thought of it myself?
  3. Is it unique?
  4. Does it fit the strategy to perfection?
  5. Could it be used for 30 years?
  If yes to all five, you may have a Big Idea. Big Ideas are rare — Ogilvy claimed no more than 20 in a career.

- **The Positively Good Doctrine** (Joel Raphaelson): — *When:* Competing with parity products. *How:* Don't claim superiority — just prove your product is genuinely good. "If you and your competitors all make excellent products, don't try to imply yours is better. Just say what's good about yours — and do a clearer, more honest, more informative job of saying it." Sales swing to whoever creates the most confidence.

- **Direct Response as Benchmark**: — *When:* Any time a general advertiser debates copy length, timing, or format. *How:* Watch what direct response advertisers do — they know dollar-for-dollar what works. They use long copy. They don't use prime time. They use two-minute commercials, not 30-second. Every copywriter should spend two years in direct response.

- **Story Appeal (Harold Rudolph)**: The magic element that makes photographs work. — *When:* Choosing or briefing photography. *How:* Inject a mystery into the visual — the reader sees it and asks "What goes on here?" then reads the copy to find out. The Hathaway eyepatch is the canonical example. The more story appeal, the higher the readership. Before-and-after photos always work; Gallup found not one before-and-after campaign that didn't increase sales.

- **Editorial Graphics**: Ads that look like editorial pages get 6× more readers than ads that look like ads. — *When:* Print layout decisions. *How:* Copy over illustration. Serif type. Three columns, 35–45 characters wide. Captions on every photo. Drop initials. Black type on white background. Never sanserif, never reverse type, never headline above the illustration (10% more people read headlines below the illustration).

- **Crown Princes (Royal Dutch Shell criteria)**: For identifying future leaders. — *When:* Hiring and talent development. *How:* Assess: (1) Power of analysis. (2) Imagination. (3) Sense of reality. (4) "Helicopter quality" — ability to see facts and problems from an overall viewpoint. Shell found these the most reliable predictors.

## Principles

- Advertising is a medium of information, not entertainment. "I don't want you to find it creative. I want you to find it so interesting that you buy the product."
- Do your homework before touching copy. Three weeks reading Rolls-Royce literature produced one line: "At sixty miles an hour, the loudest noise comes from the electric clock." That's the job.
- Positioning is the single most important decision before advertising. Make it before writing anything. Hold it for decades.
- Repeat winners. Good ads don't wear out nearly as fast as clients think. You're advertising to a moving parade, not a standing army. Run a good ad until research proves it's exhausted.
- Long copy outsells short copy for most products. "After studying retail advertising, Dr. Charles Edwards concluded that the more facts you tell, the more you sell." Direct response split-run tests prove it every time.
- Committees cannot create. They can criticize. Campaigns that look like committee minutes produce committee results. "Search the parks in all your cities / You'll find no statues of committees."
- Never use a celebrity testimonial if you can avoid it. Readers remember the celebrity, forget the product, and assume the endorsement was bought. Expert testimonials (ex-burglar praising Chubb safe) are far more persuasive.
- Specifics outperform generalities. "Sears makes a profit of 5 per cent" beats "Sears' profit is lower than you think." Numbers and specific facts are more credible and more memorable.
- The product is the hero. "There are no dull products, only dull writers." Whenever you can, make the product itself the center of the advertising. Showing the product with utter simplicity often takes more courage than any gimmick.
- Brand-building takes years. Corporate advertising that runs for 47 years (DuPont) builds measurably different results than campaigns killed in 18 months. Consistency compounds.
- Hire people who are bigger than you. "If each of us hires people who are smaller than we are, we shall become a company of dwarfs. If each of us hires people who are bigger than we are, we shall become a company of giants."

## Techniques

### Headlines
- On average, 5× more people read the headline than the body copy. A wasted headline wastes 90% of the budget.
- Lead with a benefit: "Dove creams your skin while you bathe." Count ads in any magazine that actually promise a benefit — most don't.
- News headlines are recalled 22% more: new product, improvement, new use. Use words like *introducing*, *now*, *amazing*, *suddenly* without shame — they are proven.
- Include the brand name in the headline. If you don't, 80% of readers (who skip body copy) never know what was advertised.
- Quotes around headlines increase recall by an average of 28%.
- Specifics work. "Sears makes a profit of 5 per cent." Never vague superlatives — Gallup calls that "Brag and Boast."
- Blind headlines (no product, no promise) score 20% below average. Avoid them.
- Flag target audiences: "Women over thirty-five," "Bedwetters" — narrow targeting increases relevance for the right reader.
- Avoid tricky headlines: puns, double meanings, obscurities. Your headline competes with 350 others in the average newspaper.

### Body Copy Rules
- Write to one person, not a stadium. "Pretend you are writing each of them a letter — one human being to another, second person singular."
- Use short sentences, short paragraphs, simple words. If you're unsure, take a bus to Iowa.
- Tell readers what the product will do for them — with specifics. Do not write essays.
- Write in story form. "The amazing story of a Zippo that worked after being taken from the belly of a fish."
- Avoid analogies — Gallup found them widely misunderstood. Readers don't complete the equation.
- Include price when possible. Readers who don't see price assume it's too expensive and move on.
- Use testimonials from real users (not celebrities). Have them filmed without knowing they are — their conviction is far more credible than scripted praise.

### Typography Checklist
- Never all-caps headlines — they retard reading. Use lower case with normal capitalisation.
- Never reverse type (white on black). A charity doubled fundraising by switching to black on white.
- Never superimpose headlines on illustrations.
- No period at the end of a headline — it stops the reader dead.
- Column width: 35–45 characters. Never one column of 120+ characters.
- Add drop initials to body copy: +13% readership.
- Add leading (line spacing) between paragraphs: +12% readership.
- Four times more people read captions than body copy — always caption every photo, always include brand name and promise in the caption.
- Optimal type size: 11-point.

### Print Layout
- Scan order: illustration (top) → headline (below illustration) → copy. Never put headline above illustration.
- Use two-page spreads only when the product requires horizontal display. Otherwise, two ads for the same money doubles reach.
- Make ads look like editorial pages — FCB-Impact Paris was the gold standard.

### TV Commercials — Above-Average Performers (Mapes & Ross factor analysis)
1. Humor (if the writer can actually write funny — rare)
2. Slice-of-life playlets showing product benefit
3. Real-user testimonials, especially candid/hidden camera
4. Demonstrations (dramatic proof, not dull)
5. Problem-solution format
6. Unusual/unexpected characters (+remarkably high lift in brand preference)
7. Characters from existing TV campaigns boosting print recall

### TV Commercials — Below-Average Performers
- Celebrity endorsements (high recall, low brand preference shift)
- Jingles in isolation (no sales data support)
- Abstract or "artistic" imagery without product connection

### TV Execution
- Open with the product or problem — first 5 seconds determine whether viewers stay.
- Measure brand preference change, not recall. Some high-recall commercials produce zero preference shift. Track the right metric.
- Name the brand early and often.
- Show the product in use.

## Anti-patterns

- **Artdirectoritis**: Obsessing over "cool grey bands of type," "movement," and "balance" while making copy a design element. KISS — Keep It Simple, Stupid. Drama belongs in what you say, not the typeface.
- **The Cult of Creativity**: Winning Clios while losing accounts. Of 81 TV "classics" selected by Clio, 36 agencies either lost the account or went out of business. "If it doesn't sell, it isn't creative." — Benton & Bowles.
- **Irrelevant Sex**: Showing sex unless it is functionally connected to the product. A naked woman in a cooking stove ad is a mistake. Nudes in beauty product ads are functional. Test: relevance.
- **Blind Headlines**: No product, no promise. 20% below average recall. There is no excuse.
- **Brag and Boast**: "Our product is the best in the world." Convinces nobody. Gallup named and documented the failure mode.
- **Committee Campaigns**: Trying to reconcile divergent views from too many executives produces work that achieves nothing. Most bad campaigns are minutes of a committee.
- **Premature Killing of Good Ads**: Clients and agency leaders get tired of ads long before consumers do. Henry Ford had seen the campaign so many times at meetings he thought it was over-exposed — it hadn't run yet. Run ads until research says they've worn out.
- **Originality for Its Own Sake**: "Originality is the most dangerous word in advertising." (Rosser Reeves). Preoccupied copywriters pursue swamp fire. Mozart said he never tried to compose anything original.
- **Celebrity Testimonials**: Readers remember the celebrity, forget the product, assume the celebrity was bought. Exception: expert testimonials with functional authority.
- **Reverse Type**: White on black. Dramatically reduces readership — proven across decades and countries.
- **Short TV Commercials as Default**: Direct response advertisers know two-minute commercials outperform 30-second spots. "Who, do you suppose, is more likely to be right?"
- **Ignoring Research Results**: The industry repeatedly rediscovers that reverse type is hard to read, then does it again. "Agencies waste clients' money repeating the same mistakes."
- **Corporate Slogans That Are Interchangeable**: "The resourceful company." "A company worth looking at." Any company could use any of them — and most run at the bottom of the ad where nobody reads them anyway.

## Signature Quotes

- "I don't want you to tell me that you find it 'creative.' I want you to find it so interesting that you buy the product."
- "Unless your headline sells your product, you have wasted 90 per cent of your money."
- "Big ideas come from the unconscious. Stuff your conscious mind with information, then unhook your rational thought process."
- "You cannot bore people into buying your product. You can only interest them in buying it."
- "When you reach for the stars, you may not quite get one, but you won't come up with a handful of mud either." — Leo Burnett (cited approvingly)
- "The best leaders almost always exude self-confidence. They are never petty. They are never buck-passers."
