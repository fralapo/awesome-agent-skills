## Core Frameworks

- **Definition of Comics**: "Juxtaposed pictorial and other images in deliberate sequence, intended to convey information and/or produce an aesthetic response in the viewer." The sequence and the deliberateness are load-bearing — a single image is illustration, two in sequence become comics. — *How to develop:* Before storyboarding any sequence, state its intent explicitly; let the definition discipline every panel choice.

- **The Icon / Abstraction Spectrum and the Big Triangle**: McCloud maps all imagery on a triangle whose vertices are photorealistic representation (reality), abstract language/meaning (words), and the pure picture plane (non-representational mark-making). Cartoons sit near the language vertex — they are not degraded realism but a different semiotic mode. — *How to develop:* Place every visual asset on the triangle consciously; resist defaulting to photorealism when abstraction would communicate faster or more universally.

- **Amplification Through Simplification / The Cartoon Vacuum**: Simplifying a face to a few marks amplifies its universality — the reader projects their own face onto it (the "masking effect"). Complex, rendered backgrounds can coexist with iconic characters because the character becomes a vessel for the reader's identity while the world remains external and specific. — *How to develop:* Distinguish between elements the viewer should *inhabit* (simplify) and elements they should *observe* (render); never apply the same visual register to both without intent.

- **Closure and the Gutter**: The gutter — the blank space between panels — is where comics' true action lives. Readers unconsciously complete what happens between two images: this is closure. In the gutter, human imagination takes two separate images and transforms them into a single idea — the reader's defining act of complicity. — *How to develop:* Treat every gutter as a creative decision, not empty space. Ask: what is the reader being invited to construct here? Make that construction do emotional or narrative work.

- **The Six Panel Transitions**: Moment-to-moment (minimal change, dreamy drift); action-to-action (single subject in successive actions — most economical storytelling); subject-to-subject (scene-level cuts between subjects within one idea); scene-to-scene (temporal or spatial leap, requires reader to deduce the gap); aspect-to-aspect (no time progression — wandering eye across an environment, mood, or idea; dominant in manga); non-sequitur (no logical relationship — pure juxtaposition). Western mainstream comics favour action-to-action; aspect-to-aspect carries enormous atmospheric weight. — *How to develop:* Identify the predominant transition type in any sequence you are designing. Vary deliberately; monotonous transition rhythm dulls the reader.

- **Time Frames / Space = Time**: In comics, the panel is a frozen moment but the page contains multiple moments simultaneously. A long, thin panel feels slow; a wide panel holds duration. Splash pages expand time. A single panel can contain "time" via motion lines, dialogue placement, or panel shape. — *How to develop:* Treat panel shape and size as temporal notation, not aesthetic decoration. Compress or expand time at the page design stage, not the drawing stage.

- **Word / Picture Combinations**: Seven modes — *Word-specific* (pictures illustrate but words carry narrative); *Picture-specific* (words add sound-track to self-sufficient images); *Duo-specific* (both say the same thing — the least efficient use of the medium); *Additive* (words amplify or elaborate the image); *Parallel* (words and images tell unrelated streams that never converge); *Montage* (words as integral visual elements of the image); *Interdependent* (neither words nor images fully make sense alone — together they produce a third meaning unavailable to either). The interdependent mode is uniquely comics. — *How to develop:* Name the word/picture combination mode for every panel in a rough. Replace duo-specific panels with something a single medium could not achieve.

- **The Six Steps**: McCloud's framework for what every artist navigates — *Idea/Purpose* (the why), *Form* (the chosen medium), *Idiom* (the genre or school), *Structure* (the arrangement of elements), *Craft* (the application of skill), *Surface* (the finished look). Most critical attention is spent at the surface; most determining choices are made at idea and form. — *How to develop:* Work the Six Steps top-down during conception; resist descending to surface decisions before structure decisions are locked.

- **Expressive Line / Synaesthetics**: Line quality, panel border thickness, colour temperature, and lettering style all carry emotional meaning independent of what they depict. McCloud terms the cross-sensory translation of line into emotion "synaesthetics." A jagged border makes time feel violent; a soft border makes it feel permeable. — *How to develop:* Audit the formal properties of every visual element separately from its representational content. Ask whether the form's emotional signal matches or productively contradicts the content.

## Principles

- The reader is a collaborator, not a passive receiver — the medium's power comes from what it withholds, not what it shows.
- Simplicity is not reduction; it is selective amplification of the signal that matters.
- Every formal choice (panel shape, border, gutter width, word placement) is a temporal and emotional choice, not merely aesthetic.
- Comics is a *cool* medium in McLuhan's sense — it demands completion by the reader, which generates identification and investment.
- The history of comics is a history of transition-type choices; understanding what each culture reaches for reveals its storytelling values.
- Seeing is not neutral — the rendered world is observed, the iconic character is inhabited.
- Comics is the only medium that makes *space* and *time* the same variable on the page.
- The gutter is not absence; it is the medium's primary site of meaning-making.
- Form and content cannot be separated without loss — the shape of the container changes the shape of what it holds.
- Mastery of the medium requires being conscious of all six steps simultaneously, not just the surface ones.
- Language and image are not opposed; they exist on a continuum and the most powerful comics exploit the full range.

## Techniques

- **Transition Mapping**: Before finalising a sequence, label every panel-to-panel transition by type. If 80% are action-to-action, that is a structural fact, not an accident — decide whether it is the right fact.
- **Gutter Staging**: Identify the three most important off-panel events in any narrative. Stage the panels that bracket them so the gutter "contains" the right emotional material for the reader to complete.
- **Masking Deployment**: Use a simplified iconic register for point-of-view or protagonist characters; reserve rendered specificity for the world, antagonists, or objects the protagonist confronts.
- **Panel Shape as Tempo**: Design page layouts with explicit temporal intent — wide panels for held moments, tall narrow panels for rapid succession, borderless for timelessness or dream.
- **Word/Picture Mode Audit**: For every page, verify that at least some panels use the interdependent mode. Eliminate all duo-specific combinations unless they serve irony or emphasis.
- **Six-Step Top-Down**: Lock idea, form, and idiom before touching structure; lock structure before touching craft; address surface last.
- **Synaesthetic Consistency**: Choose a line quality and lettering register for each emotional register in the work and maintain them as a system — let formal variables carry meaning the reader learns to read subconsciously.
- **Aspect-to-Aspect for Atmosphere**: When a scene needs to establish mood rather than advance plot, substitute one or two aspect-to-aspect sequences for action-to-action — force the reader to inhabit the environment before the story moves.

## Anti-patterns

- **Duo-specific redundancy**: Captioning what is already fully visible in the image. The medium's power lies in divergence between word and image channels; redundancy wastes both.
- **Uniform transition monotony**: Using only action-to-action transitions throughout signals a failure to think about rhythm. Readers disengage from mechanically regular sequences.
- **Rendering everything equally**: Applying the same level of visual detail to characters, environments, and objects destroys the masking effect and tells the reader what to observe rather than whom to become.
- **Treating the gutter as nothing**: Designing only what appears in panels while leaving gutter implications to chance. The implied event is as crafted a choice as the depicted one.
- **Conflating surface with form**: Spending creative attention on rendering style while leaving panel structure, transition logic, and word/picture relationships unexamined. Surface is the least determining layer.
- **Non-sequitur without purpose**: Random juxtaposition that produces no productive ambiguity or third meaning — pure noise. Non-sequitur transitions are valid only when the reader's uncertainty is the intended experience.
- **Ignoring time as a page design variable**: Treating panel size and shape as compositional convenience rather than temporal notation. Every layout decision is a decision about how time feels.
