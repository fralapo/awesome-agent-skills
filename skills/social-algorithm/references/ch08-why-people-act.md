# Chapter 8: Why People Act — The CTA Is Not the Lever

## Core Idea
The call to action is not what makes people act. It is a **gentle nudge that only works when the prerequisites and the structure already exist**. What actually drives action is content that makes the prospect **perceive a need** for you and a **gap** between where they are and where they could get with you. Build that, and the CTA becomes optional — people reach the link in bio and fill the form even when you never ask.

---

## 1. CTAs No Longer Work (and Will Work Less and Less)

- Standard CTAs are burned out by overuse: "comment below", "let me know what you think", "DM me info". The audience has seen them thousands of times and no longer reacts.
- Pasting a CTA at the end of a piece does **not** by itself produce qualified chats or booked calls.
- This is a structural decline, not a tweak you can copy-fix: the market is too used to this mode of communication.

**Reframe**: stop asking "what CTA do I put at the end?" Start asking "how do I structure content that makes things happen?"

---

## 2. Why Humans Take an Action

We act when we **feel a need**.

The only real reason a person will ever DM you to ask about your service is that your communication made them **perceive a need** for you and for what you do. It is an **emotional sensation**, not a logical decision.

**The purchase process**: think of how you normally buy. On a marketplace you buy what you feel you need (sometimes even what you objectively don't need, but feel you want). Services and content work the same way — purchase is moved by felt need, not by the prompt at the end.

So the real question is not *which CTA*, but: **how do I create content that generates the sensation of needing me or what I offer?** That sensation is what moves the purchase.

---

## 3. The Real Problem Is Not the CTA

Common failure mode: people insert the CTA but **forget to talk about the service** — the audience doesn't even know precisely what they do. Of course it doesn't work.

> It is often not a sales problem and not a content problem. **The prerequisites for action are missing.**

Sales content can be **correct on paper** — respect every rule of the format — and still move no one, because it doesn't create need. A flawless-looking sales Story that "doesn't stir anything" produces zero replies.

**The variable of action is the content, not the CTA.** The CTA is a gentle push that only works if the structure underneath has already created need. With the right structure you can drop the CTA entirely and people still act.

---

## 4. The Structure — Two Reasoning Frames

Two frames build the need that makes any CTA (optional) actually convert.

### Frame 1 — The Real Reasons People Pay You
Be brutally clear on **why someone would give you their money**.

- "Take the client's pains" is not enough — there are too many pains, too many situations. You must find the **core**: the real reason it makes sense for them to invest time and money.
- Ask: **which situations does the prospect want to manage or solve with your help?** Not every real problem is a *buying* problem.

> Worked example: a coaching-niche Story about managing anger ("in the past I'd have gotten furious, now I handle it") was correct on paper and opened with a poll — but it moved no one. Anger management is a real problem, but it is **not the real reason someone would pay**. Re-running the question "what is the principal reason I'd come to you and invest?" surfaced a stronger lever — **procrastination** — because it is a tangible, recurring consequence in the person's life. That theme is the one that carries toward the sale.

→ Step 1 is to **identify the right levers** — the real reasons you are useful to people. This is the principle of need applied: people buy when they need you; found the sale on *why* they need you.

### Frame 2 — Desire Over Pain
Once you have the levers, **do not start from making people feel bad.**

- Classic marketing says: touch the pains, the cords, the triggers. Pains matter — but they are not the main lever on organic.
- What converts more on organic content is working on **desire** (the *desiderata*): making people understand **where they could get to**, the desired situation.

**Stories that convert most** — even with no CTA — describe the **client's situation and the results obtained**. Why: you let the viewer see that **someone in their situation is getting radically different results**, which creates the healthy gap:

> "How on earth did they do it?"

That question is exactly what the prospect must ask **before** taking an action toward you. It is the gap + desire that powers the nudge.

---

## 5. The Final Diagnosis

When someone says "the CTAs don't work" — it is **not the CTAs**. It is communication that **does not create need** for them and for their service.

Fix the communication (the two frames), not the prompt at the end.

---

## Anti-Patterns
- **CTA at the end as the lever** → it is only a nudge; without prerequisites it fails
- **CTA on content that never explains the service** → audience doesn't know what you do, so the ask lands on nothing
- **"Correct on paper" sales Story** → respects every format rule but creates no need → zero replies
- **Twisting the knife in the pain** → not the organic lever; over-relying on pain underperforms desire
- **Picking any real problem as the hook** → a real problem ≠ a *buying* problem; you need the core lever
- **Blaming the CTA for low conversion** → misdiagnosis; the communication isn't creating need

## Key Takeaways
1. People act when they feel a need; the CTA only triggers a need that already exists
2. The real question is not "which CTA" but "how do I create the sensation of needing me / my offer"
3. Low conversion is usually missing prerequisites, not a weak CTA
4. Frame 1: get brutally clear on the real reason people pay you — the core lever, not every pain
5. Not every real problem is a buying problem — find the situation worth paying to solve
6. Frame 2: lead with desire (where they could get) over pain (twisting the knife)
7. Result/situation Stories convert best — they create the "how did they do it?" gap
8. With both frames in place the CTA is optional; people reach the link and act unprompted
