## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

### The Value Proposition Template (Ch5)
"For **[target customer]**, dissatisfied with **[current alternative]**, our product provides **[differentiated capability]** that, unlike **[named alternatives]**, does **[something unique]**." Precision on "for who" is a feature, not a limitation — vague targets make go-to-market, messaging, and channel choice impossible.

### The Four U's — is this problem worth solving? (Ch5)
**Unworkable** (broken enough that someone gets fired if it isn't fixed) · **Unavoidable** (structurally inescapable — tax, regulation, aging) · **Urgent** (relative to the customer's *other* priorities, not absolute — ask "what's your #1 priority right now?") · **Underserved** (real white space, not 10 existing vendors). Consumer-market variant: **BLAC** — Blatant/Latent × Aspirational/Critical; best target = blatant + critical.

### The Three D's — is this a breakthrough, not an incremental idea? (Ch5)
**Discontinuous** (non-linear leap, not faster/better/cheaper) + **Disruptive** (changes the basis of competition, often via business model) + **Defensible** (protectable via IP, structure, or proprietary advantage). A disruptive business model can beat a technically superior product.

### CORE and Minimum Viable Segment (Ch5)
**CORE** = the single word/reason a customer would give, reflexively, for why they use you — define it before choosing a monetization model; it's usually narrower than founders think. **MVS** = the smallest group of users sharing one identical need — the "dance partner" of an MVP. Win it completely before broadening.

### Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning (Ch2)
A segment must be large enough, reachable, homogeneous in needs, and responsive to the marketing mix similarly — and, critically, **actionable**. Positioning = the space a brand occupies in the customer's mind; identify points of parity (table stakes) before points of difference (what wins). Perceptual maps synthesize aggregate customer perception, not objective fact.

### Strategy vs. Planning (Ch4)
Planning lists comfortable, controllable activities with no theory of winning. Strategy is a coherent, falsifiable theory: where we play, how we win, what capabilities/systems that requires — an outcome you influence but don't control. Escape the "planning trap" by writing the logic explicitly: "what would have to be true about ourselves, the industry, competitors, and customers for this to work?"

### Porter's Five Forces (Ch4)
Rivalry, buyer power, supplier power, threat of new entrants, threat of substitutes — a structural cap on industry profitability independent of execution quality. Diagnose the industry boundary correctly first; treat the forces as dynamic, not a static snapshot.

### Pricing Strategy Selector (Ch3)
Skimming (recoup R&D, quality signal, barriers to entry) vs. penetration (fast volume, capacity ready) vs. prestige (luxury/status) vs. cost-plus (B2B partner sustainability). Skimming → penetration works; the reverse rarely does, because customers anchor to a low launch price. Always compare total profit **dollars**, not margin percentage.

### Product → Company Roadmap (Ch6)
Classify what you have — feature, product, or company — before investing further. The Product–Company Gap is a go-to-market and business-model problem, not an engineering one. R&D-to-S&M spend inverts as a company matures (~100% product at founding → S&M several multiples of R&D at scale); budget go-to-market at ~2x product-development cost from day one.

### Founder Readiness (Ch6)
"Last day of work" test · timing inequality (fear of too-late > fear of too-early) · Rich vs. King (money or control — rarely both) · the 7–10 year horizon, not a 6-month "flip it" mentality. Founders can't scale by default — the skillset that starts a company differs from what scaling requires; audit where your time actually goes.

### The Sales Pipeline & 4 Pillars (Ch7)
Leads → Suspects (MQL) → Prospects (SQL) → Opportunities → Customers, with rising conversion ratios left-to-right. Every B2B conversation sequences strictly: **Empathy → Trust → Value → Confidence** — jumping to competence/fix-mode first is a fatal error.

### Principled Negotiation — Getting to Yes (Ch7)
Interests, not positions → objective standards for residual conflict → options for mutual gain, brainstormed before evaluation → people separate from the problem. Know your **BATNA** before you start.
