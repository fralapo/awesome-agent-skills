# Chapter 2: Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning & Branding

## Market Segmentation
- **Segmentation**: dividing a total market into submarkets (segments) of potential customers more alike than the market as a whole. Segments are derived from research (primary/secondary, qualitative/quantitative) — never guessed, then named internally.
- **Four criteria for a viable segment**: (1) large enough to be worth pursuing; (2) reachable — exposable to a marketing communications plan via specific media/channels; (3) homogeneous needs and wants within the segment; (4) responds to the marketing mix similarly (same price sensitivity, channel preference, messaging reaction). A segment rarely satisfies all four for 100% of members — a "significant percentage" is sufficient. A segment must be **actionable**, not merely "interesting."
- **Segmentation bases**: Demographic (age, gender, race, religion, income, education, occupation), Geographic (region/country/city — narrower geography = stronger homogeneity assumption), Psychographic (lifestyle, life stage, interests, attitudes), Behavioral (usage rate: light/moderate/heavy/non-user; product-benefit sought; use occasion).
- **Benefit segmentation**: group customers by the specific benefit they seek (toothpaste: cavity prevention, whitening, fresh breath, tartar control). An "all-in-one" product spanning every benefit dilutes the message — customers seeking one specific benefit want it emphasized, not buried among five claims.
- **Usage-rate segmentation**: heavy users deserve disproportionate marketing spend to retain them (more efficient than acquiring new users) — message them with reassurance, not feature education. For light/non-users, diagnose *why* (price, taste, health perception, lack of awareness) before choosing a lever (reformulate, reprice, re-message).
- **Use occasion**: the situational context of use. A brand can grow usage by expanding the use occasion (repositioning a "breakfast-only" drink as anytime).
- **Target market vs. target audience**: target market = everyone the company wants to buy the product (can be broad — "all women 18–88"); target audience = the narrower subset a specific campaign is designed to reach. Creative (talent, imagery, messaging, media placement) must be customized to the target audience. **Multicultural marketing** runs distinct creative executions per audience rather than one-size-fits-all, because cultural subgroups within a demographic band can have different needs despite superficial similarity.
- **Advertising waste**: no medium reaches only the target audience; media planning's job is minimizing waste ("I know 50% of my advertising is wasted, I just don't know which 50%").
- **Market sizing**: quantify each segment's size (units/dollars/population%) after segmenting, before allocating resources — always define the denominator (what market, what geography) or a percentage is meaningless.
- **Targeting**: select which segment(s) to penetrate — full-market penetration is rarely practical. Selection criteria: segment size, growth rate, overall attractiveness, concentration, cost to penetrate. Companies reward **focus** (best-at-one-thing) over serving every segment — even large companies deliberately stay out of adjacent categories to protect focus.
- **Market concentration**: a market is "concentrated" when few competitors control most share vs. "fragmented" (many competitors sharing similar share) — affects competitive intensity and attractiveness.
- **Minimum Viable Segment (MVS)**: the smallest group of users sharing the *same* underlying need such that one product design serves all of them without modification — the "dance partner" of an MVP. Deserves as much design attention as MVP functionality; a good segment is 5+ customers sharing the exact same need — win the first and you can systematically win the next four. Keep drilling down (e.g., "field workers" → "services people," not "salespeople") until the need is genuinely homogeneous.
- **Big Market, Small Segment**: define a narrow beachhead segment inside a large market — sharper segment definition makes product-market fit, packaging/pricing, channel choice, and messaging dramatically more efficient with limited resources. Starting "too broad" is one of the most common startup failure modes.

## Positioning & Perceptual Mapping
- **Positioning**: the space a brand occupies in the customer's mind relative to competitors, based on key category dimensions (commonly price and quality, but also innovation, reliability, durability, ease of use). A brand cannot be "all things to all people" — it must choose a position.
- **Perceptual map**: a graphic (typically 2-axis) representation of brand positioning relative to competitors, built from branding/attitude research. Practitioners typically build 10–12 maps across different dimension pairs, because no single map captures all relevant positioning. It synthesizes an aggregate customer-perception view, not objective fact.
- **Points of parity vs. points of difference**: points of parity = minimum table-stakes features shared with competitors (necessary but not differentiating — identify these first). Points of difference = unique features that differentiate and justify a price premium — the basis of the unique selling proposition (USP). Too much difference from category norms can suppress purchase — excessive deviation undermines trust and pushes buyers back to a safer, conventional alternative.
- **Positioning 2×2 matrices**: the choice of axes is the critical design decision. Generic axes ("faster/better") are weak; choose axes that expose a real structural barrier competitors can't easily cross.
- **Repositioning**: if research shows a brand's market perception doesn't match desired positioning, it can be deliberately repositioned via advertising, sampling, testimonials, or celebrity endorsement — but this isn't done frequently; positioning must stay clear over time. Unintentional repositioning can occur as a side effect of pricing/line decisions (a luxury brand introducing lower price points erodes prestige perception). Repositioning should be justified by a clear metric-based reason (declining awareness/attitude/share), not done reflexively.
- **Brand tracking research**: measure brand awareness (recognition/recall) and perception/attitude shifts over time to judge whether a campaign changed the targeted perception.
- **White Space**: find the unique position competitors have left open through rigorous targeting/segmentation, not through broad claims.
- **Brand Essence progression**: different → better → unique. "Different" and "better" are necessary but insufficient; durable brand value requires genuine uniqueness.
- **Emotional connection / Brand Promise**: beyond functional claims, articulate what the product enables someone to become or achieve (aspirational/identity-level benefit), not just feature claims.

## Branding & Brand Architecture
- **Brand vs. product**: the product delivers the same generic functionality across a category; the brand wrapped around it differentiates, communicates value, and carries personality. Strong brands enable price premiums.
- **Branding elements**: logo (word mark), symbol (non-word mark), brand name, tagline, packaging, trade dress (color, materials, shape). Four criteria for brand identity elements: memorable, protectable, adaptable, transferable.
- **Tagline vs. slogan**: tagline = short, enduring phrase tied permanently to the brand, embodying its positioning/USP (rarely changes). Slogan = the theme for a specific campaign, changed regularly to teach new messages and combat advertising wear-out.
- **Trademark protection**: a branding element must be unique/protectable; generic dictionary words generally can't be trademarked as brand names (though a stylized logo built around such a name can be). Companies sometimes invent novel words specifically to secure trademark protection. Brand names must be checked for meaning/connotation in each target market (e.g., a name translating poorly in another language).
- **Brand strategy types**: **Multi-product/family branding**: one brand name across many categories (e.g., Sony across TVs, consoles, DVD players). **Multi-brand (house of brands)**: different brand names per product from one parent (e.g., Tide, Crest, Scope, Gain from one company). **Private branding/label**: a retailer's own exclusive brand — sometimes the same manufacturer produces both a national brand and the private label.
- **Brand hierarchy**: corporate brand → master brand(s) → sub-brands. Sub-brands borrow the master brand's equity/awareness — pairing a new/unknown name with a high-equity master brand transfers trust instantly.
- **Brand elasticity / brand stretch limits**: how far a brand name can be credibly stretched into adjacent categories. Extension works when there's a logical/emotional connection to the core brand meaning; a brand can sell across multiple price points but not so wide a range that it damages premium perception — when the range needs to push further, introduce a new master brand instead (e.g., Toyota → Lexus, rather than a $55K Toyota-badged sedan).
- **Trade dress / packaging as "silent salesperson"**: packaging and recognizable visual elements accumulate equity over time; changing them abruptly can strip away accumulated recognition and trigger backlash even when the product is unchanged. A redesign should be justified by a clear metric-based reason.
- **Advertising vs. publicity**: advertising is paid, and the company fully controls the message; publicity is earned/free but uncontrolled — it can be positive or negative and carries reputational risk.
- **Celebrity endorsement risk**: celebrities transfer credibility/aspiration but introduce disconnect risk (scandal, polarizing appeal, overexposure diluting authenticity). Selection should weigh target-audience fit over sheer fame.
- **"Annoying but memorable" advertising**: creative that irritates can still build recall effectively, as long as the negative association stays with the ad execution and doesn't transfer to perceived product quality.
- **Brand awareness metrics**: brand recognition (aided) vs. brand recall (unaided). "Top of mind" = first brand recalled. Awareness alone is insufficient — must also measure purchase intent and consideration set; an acquired/relaunched brand can retain high awareness but lose relevance.
- **Brand equity**: brand value can be a massive intangible asset independent of tangible assets (a global brand valued at tens of billions based purely on brand strength).

## Competitive Set
- **Direct vs. indirect competitors**: direct = substitutable products in the same immediate category (different orange juice brands); indirect = serve the same underlying need via a different category (orange juice vs. milk for calcium/vitamins). Classify indirect competitors explicitly so they aren't forgotten — an indirect competitor can become direct overnight. Category-level advertising ("Got Milk?") signals a company views a different category as its true competitive threat.
- **Primary demand vs. selective demand advertising**: primary-demand advertising grows the whole category (rare, typically industry/trade-association-funded, since within-category rivals cooperate against a shared substitute threat); selective-demand advertising grows one brand's share within an already-demanded category (most advertising).
- **Competing on the same value proposition, not the same label**: firms that are technically different (different menu, different sub-industry) can still be direct competitors if they serve the same need at the same price/occasion.
- **Define your competitive set by the resource actually being fought over**, not the category label: e.g., competing for a broad pool of a customer's disposable time and attention (vs. same-category rivals only) is often a more accurate competitive frame than category label alone — it changes what "market share" and "competitor" even mean.
