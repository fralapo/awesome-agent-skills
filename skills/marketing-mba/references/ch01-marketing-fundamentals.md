# Chapter 1: Marketing Fundamentals, Planning & Product Lifecycle

## Core Definitions & Process
- **Marketing**: creating, communicating, and delivering value. Value is a function of price, quality, features, and benefits.
- **Marketing concept**: identify and satisfy an unmet need while simultaneously achieving organizational goals (market share, sales, profitability, customer satisfaction) — not need-satisfaction alone.
- **Requirements for marketing to occur**: two or more parties with unsatisfied needs, ability to satisfy them, a way to communicate, and something to exchange.
- **Five Key Marketing Activities** (sequential): (1) identify an unmet need via research, (2) develop a concept, (3) determine a price the customer is willing to pay, (4) gain distribution, (5) build awareness. Distribution must precede advertising — spending on awareness before the product is on shelf wastes money and risks stockouts.
- **AIDA model**: advertising must first get Attention (stopping power), create Interest, build Desire, and drive Action (purchase). Requires meaningful investment and repeated exposure (frequency) to be decoded, learned, and remembered.
- **Stakeholders vs. shareholders**: a stakeholder is anyone with an interest in the organization (chairman, shareholders, suppliers, employees, customers); a shareholder is one type of stakeholder — never conflate the terms.
- **Relationship marketing / CRM**: linking the organization to individual customers, employees, suppliers, and other stakeholders for mutual long-term benefit (e.g., loyalty/points programs).
- **Pricing/organizational objectives beyond profit**: sales (dollar or unit), market share, and social responsibility (e.g., pricing medication affordably) are all legitimate primary objectives.

## Corporate, Business & Functional Planning
- **Three levels of organizational planning, operating simultaneously**: (1) Corporate plan — senior management; defines mission, vision, values. (2) Business plan — Strategic Business Units (SBUs)/divisions; operationalizes the corporate mission into reality. (3) Functional plan — departments (marketing, manufacturing); implements the business plan tactically. All three must share the same objectives, though tactics vary.
- **Mission vs. vision**: Mission = the business the company is in now, present-tense, short enough that every employee can recite it. Vision = where the organization aspires to be in the future. Missions adjust as goals are achieved; a fulfilled vision can become the new mission.
- **Values**: what the organization stands for; must be substantiated by real action, not website language. Test whether a value is real: would the company actually hire or fire someone based on adherence to it, independent of job performance? A value only counts if it survives contact with a business trade-off.
- **Mantra over mission statement (startup-stage alternative)**: a long, consultant-crafted mission statement is typically vague and unusable by employees. A 3–4 word mantra (e.g., "authentic athletic performance," "democratize commerce," "peace of mind") captures the organization's essence for employees and customers alike. Save formal mission statements for later-stage, large organizations.
- **Culture is the one thing not to iterate**: everything else (product, value prop, business model) should pivot; culture should be set from day one and held constant — instilling culture after ~30–40 people is very hard. Culture = guiding principles (convictions on how you treat customers/employees/partners) + values (empowering, not restrictive rules).
- **SWOT analysis**: Strengths/Weaknesses = internal; Opportunities/Threats = external. The purpose of identifying weaknesses is corrective (convert into strengths), not just diagnostic. Apply to your own organization and to competitors. Should be summarizable as a one-page matrix. Information/analysis is only "potential power" — it becomes actual power only when acted upon (e.g., Harley-Davidson used SWOT-driven corrective action to recover market share from ~23% to ~65%).

## Growth & Portfolio Strategy
- **Core growth strategy options**: market penetration, market development, diversification, and new product development.
- **Related vs. unrelated diversification**: related = expanding into adjacent categories serving overlapping needs (a soft-drink company acquiring a juice brand); unrelated = acquiring businesses with no functional connection to the core. Capital markets increasingly reward focus over diversification; the primary disadvantage of unrelated diversification is loss of managerial focus/expertise.
- **Diversification risk lesson**: using a mature, high-share "cash cow" to fund diversification into unrelated businesses can dilute focus and starve the core of investment; companies often eventually retrench to core competencies. Merging two structurally weak companies is a survival strategy, not guaranteed synergy — success requires complementary strengths/weaknesses, not just scale.
- **Horizontal vs. vertical integration**: horizontal = expanding within the same value-chain level into different categories (a footwear retailer opening electronics stores). Vertical integration = a manufacturer opening its own retail stores, capturing both manufacturing and retail margin while still typically also selling through third-party retailers.
- **M&A rationale/risk**: successful combinations require complementary competencies (your weakness is their strength); redundant back-office functions can be consolidated for efficiency, but culture clash and "who's the boss" power struggles are the biggest integration risks, typically resolved by relative asset size/valuation.
- **BCG Matrix**: classify SBUs/product lines by industry growth rate × relative market share.
  - **Star**: high growth, high share — requires continued heavy investment to defend.
  - **Cash Cow**: low growth, high share — generates surplus profit, often reallocated to fund Stars; "milking" too long erodes share.
  - **Question Mark**: high growth, low share — decision point: invest heavily to build share, or divest/harvest.
  - **Dog**: low growth, low share — minimal investment, candidate for divestment.
  - High growth in this model means ~50–300% category growth, not 2–3%.
- **Cannibalization vs. incrementality**: incremental sales = new revenue that wouldn't otherwise exist; cannibalization = new product substituting for the company's own existing sales. The "cost of doing nothing" is real — if you don't cannibalize yourself, a competitor captures that substitute demand instead. Successive product versions are largely acceptable cannibalization ("continuous innovation"), not a problem.
- **SKU rationalization**: recurring portfolio-pruning exercise (often reveals ~20% of SKUs generate ~80% of sales); requires judgment about newly launched items vs. long-standing underperformers.

## Products & Product Classification
- **Generic functionality vs. brand**: all products in a category share the same generic functional benefit (all cars provide transportation); the brand wrapped around the product is what differentiates.
- **Durable vs. non-durable (consumable) goods**: durable = reusable across many uses (may still wear out); non-durable = used up and must be replenished. Consumable purchase cycles are short; durable repurchase cycles are long — marketing's objective for durables is to shorten the interval between initial and repeat purchase.
- **Convenience / shopping / specialty / unsought products**:
  - **Convenience**: low involvement, impulse-driven, requires intensive/ubiquitous distribution.
  - **Shopping**: customer actively compares across stores/brands; moderate/high involvement; selective distribution.
  - **Specialty**: customer aggressively seeks out the specific product (antiques, luxury cars); deliberately limited distribution — scarcity supports the position.
  - **Unsought**: not top-of-mind or actively searched for (burial plots, some insurance); requires push marketing/education to build initial awareness.
- **Utility types**: form utility (product design/packaging), time utility, and place utility (available where/when the customer needs it).
- **Product line vs. product mix**: a product line is a group of related items; a product mix is the full set of a company's product lines.
- **Business product types**: component (raw material/part incorporated into the buyer's finished product) vs. support product (consumed in operations but not incorporated into output).

## New Product Development
- **Five-stage NPD process**: (1) identify an opportunity/unmet need via research, (2) develop a concept (concept board tested with consumers, refined over rounds), (3) develop the product (prototype tested for touch/feel), (4) manage the launch, (5) track results. Each stage is a go/no-go gate — a project can be terminated even after meaningful sunk investment, because continuing a bad project costs more than stopping it.
- **Primary vs. secondary research**: primary = initiated/commissioned by the company for its own use (even if outsourced); secondary = pre-existing research purchased from third parties (cheap because costs are spread across many buyers).
- **Concept testing process**: concept boards (visual + written statement) shown to consumers across successive rounds, revised each round, before investing in costlier physical prototypes.
- **Reasons new products fail**: poor marketing-mix execution (a mediocre plan flawlessly executed can outperform a brilliant plan poorly executed); failure to reach the target audience with enough reach/frequency; "me-too" products lacking a point of difference; bad timing; lack of brand-promise credibility (claims too extreme to back up); ineffective communication; failure to secure retail distribution (must convince the salespeople, then the retailer, then the consumer); too much entrenched competition; category maturity.
- **Shelf-space/planogram economics**: retailers evaluate a new item by the incremental sales/margin it will generate for that shelf section, because adding one item requires removing another (fixed shelf space). Limited/test distribution is common before full chain-wide rollout.

## Product Life Cycle & Diffusion of Innovation
- **PLC stages**: Introduction (sales zero) → Growth → Maturity (sales plateau; 2–3%/year still counts as "mature," not decline) → Decline → potential obsolescence. The life cycle is not deterministic — marketers actively extend growth by modifying the marketing mix (price, features, line/brand extensions) rather than accepting decline passively; "doing nothing" carries a real cost.
- **Two ways to grow sales in a mature (flat) category**: (1) grow the entire category (add features/benefits, launch extensions that expand total category volume), or (2) steal share from competitors (zero-sum within a flat category).
- **Line extension vs. brand extension**: line extension = new variant within the same category, same brand (new flavor). Brand extension = extending the brand name into a new category (Apple: computers → MP3 players → phones → tablets).
- **Diffusion of Innovation (Rogers)**: adoption follows a bell curve — Innovators (~2.5–5%), Early Adopters, Early Majority (~34%), Late Majority (~34%), Laggards. Price, advertising intensity, and added features are the main levers to accelerate movement along the curve; a competitor won't wait for you to sit on a strategy.
- **First-mover advantage**: being first to market is often more valuable than being better later (first MP3 players existed before iPod, but Apple's execution won the category) — but first-mover advantage attracts fast-following competitors once profitability is visible.
- **Skimming vs. penetration** (see also Chapter 3, Pricing): skimming captures innovators at a high price first; penetration prices low to drive volume/adoption quickly, but requires production capacity sufficient to meet resulting demand and risks a leapfrogging competitor before that capacity pays off.
- **Fixed-cost trap**: a market-share leader that builds massive fixed manufacturing capacity in a growth era becomes structurally disadvantaged when competitors erode share, because fixed costs must be absorbed over fewer units, raising per-unit cost.

## Consumer Behavior & Perception
- **High-involvement vs. low-involvement purchases**: high-involvement categories (expensive electronics) generate customer persistence (they'll return later if out of stock); low-involvement categories risk permanent lost sales on a stockout.
- **Perceptual filters**: **Perceptual vigilance** (we only attend to stimuli relevant to current needs — ignoring food ads when not hungry); **Perceptual defense** (we block messaging that makes us uncomfortable, e.g., smokers ignoring cancer warnings — such messaging may better target non-smokers/families); **Adaptation** (repeated exposure to the same ad causes people to stop noticing it — "advertising wear-out," which is why campaigns/slogans are refreshed regularly).
- **Evoked set vs. consideration set**: evoked set = all brands that come to mind in a category (raw awareness); consideration set = the smaller subset seriously considered for purchase. Awareness research must probe purchase intent and consideration set, not just awareness.
- **Aided vs. unaided awareness**: aided = prompted recognition; unaided = spontaneous recall — a harder, more valuable form requiring greater ad spend/exposure. "Top of mind awareness" = the first brand recalled in a category, the most enviable position.
- **Purchase decision roles**: influencer, decision-maker, purchaser, and end user can be different people (e.g., marketing a toy to both the child who plays with it and the parent who buys it).
- **Perceived risk types**: financial risk (price) and social risk (fear of disapproval/status loss) — sales promotions (coupons, rebates) primarily reduce financial risk to drive trial.
- **Post-purchase dissonance ("buyer's remorse")**: doubt experienced after a purchase, especially higher-involvement ones. Marketing to heavy/existing users should focus on reassurance/reinforcement (confirming they made the right choice) rather than repeating basic feature education aimed at new/light users.

## Marketing Research Methods
- **Qualitative vs. quantitative research**: qualitative (focus groups of ~10–12 people; several rounds only reach ~48 people total) generates directional insight but isn't statistically significant. Quantitative research (surveys of ~1,500 respondents) validates qualitative findings with a statistically significant, representative sample.
- **Primary vs. secondary research**: primary = initiated specifically for the company's own decision; secondary = pre-existing reports purchased off the shelf.
- **Branding/awareness research** should be tracked longitudinally (every 6 months to years) to measure trend changes — a single snapshot ("20% aware") is far less meaningful than the trend over time (growing from 20% to 40%).
