# Patterns — Techniques & Playbooks

## Value Prop Validation ("Why do you keep giving us money?")
**When to use**: after initial traction, when the team's assumed value proposition may not match why customers actually buy.
**How**: don't accept a customer's first, polite answer about why they buy. Keep asking — "peel the onion" — until you find the real, specific differentiator driving retention.
**Trade-offs**: takes discipline to keep pushing past comfortable answers; the real answer is sometimes uncomfortable (it may not be the thing the team is proudest of).

## The Four U's Diagnostic
**When to use**: evaluating whether a candidate problem is worth building a company around.
**How**: score the problem on Unworkable / Unavoidable / Urgent / Underserved. Ask "who gets fired if this doesn't get fixed?" to test Unworkable. Ask "what's your #1 priority right now?" to test Urgent (never lead with your pitch).
**Trade-offs**: a problem strong on only one or two U's can still work but needs a correspondingly stronger execution/team story to compensate.

## Narrow-First-Segment (Minimum Viable Segment)
**When to use**: defining an initial go-to-market target.
**How**: find 5+ customers sharing the exact same underlying need; keep drilling down until the need is genuinely homogeneous (e.g., "field service workers," not "salespeople"). Win that segment completely before expanding.
**Trade-offs**: feels like leaving money on the table early; the payoff is sharper messaging, channel choice, and product-market fit signal.

## Before/After (Pain → Joy) Narrative
**When to use**: qualitatively evaluating or pitching a value proposition.
**How**: describe the customer's concrete state before your solution (acute, specific pain) and after (concrete relief/joy). Classify the solution as "vitamin" (skippable) or "penicillin" (painful to be without) — aim for penicillin.
**Trade-offs**: requires real customer research to fill in specifics; a generic before/after ("saves time") is not diagnostic.

## Bottom-Up Market Sizing
**When to use**: any investor pitch or internal business case involving a revenue projection.
**How**: build from website visitors → conversion rate → units sold, not "1% of a huge market."
**Trade-offs**: harder to construct than a top-down guess; produces credible numbers investors actually trust, and top-down "1%" framing reads as an admission of weak penetration.

## Founder-Led Top-of-Funnel
**When to use**: pre-product-market-fit, before hiring a dedicated salesperson.
**How**: the founder personally owns suspect/prospect qualification (the low-value top of funnel); only escalate to team time once a lead becomes a genuine opportunity. Hire the first salesperson only once the ideal customer profile is proven repeatable ("100 more customers just like these 12").
**Trade-offs**: doesn't scale — it's a deliberate, temporary constraint to force founder-market learning before delegating.

## Four Pillars Sales Sequencing
**When to use**: any B2B sales conversation, especially early-stage/high-stakes deals.
**How**: sequence strictly — Empathy (show you understand their pain) → Trust (show you have their interest in mind) → Value (in their terms) → Confidence (only now, your product/team). Never open with competence/fix-mode.
**Trade-offs**: slower than jumping straight to a pitch; the payoff is avoiding the "fatal error" of reading as dismissive.

## Principled Negotiation (Getting to Yes)
**When to use**: any negotiation where an ongoing relationship matters (co-founder splits, partnership terms, vendor contracts).
**How**: (1) surface interests behind stated positions via repeated "why"; (2) resolve residual conflict with objective external standards, not personal preference; (3) brainstorm options for mutual gain before evaluating any of them; (4) be soft on the person, hard on the problem. Establish your BATNA before the negotiation starts.
**Trade-offs**: slower and more effortful than a quick win-lose haggle; produces more durable agreements and preserves the relationship.

## MAT — Milestones, Assumptions, Tasks
**When to use**: prioritizing early-stage execution when everything feels urgent.
**How**: classify every candidate activity as a Milestone (raises valuation), an Assumption (a numeric bet that needs testing), or a Task (only exists to complete a milestone/test an assumption). Discard anything that's none of these (e.g., touring office furniture).
**Trade-offs**: requires discipline to say no to comfortable, non-milestone activity.

## Pain/Gain-Ratio Sales-Cycle Design
**When to use**: designing marketing/sales collateral and proof points across a buying cycle.
**How**: for each stage of the buyer's journey, explicitly design content to maximize perceived gain and minimize perceived pain/effort of the next step. Remember the company only controls the "gears" (process/tools); the customer controls the "accelerator, brake, clutch" (their own pace).
**Trade-offs**: requires mapping the full buying cycle honestly, including stages the seller doesn't control.

## Fear-Setting (Ferriss)
**When to use**: any postponed, high-stakes personal or business decision.
**How**: write three columns — worst-case outcomes, mitigations for each, and the recovery path if the worst happens.
**Trade-offs**: takes 15–30 minutes of deliberate discomfort; routinely reveals the downside is bounded/reversible.

## Green-Product Credibility Framework
**When to use**: launching or repositioning a product with a sustainability claim.
**How**: hit all four — performance parity with the category leader, distribution parity (sold where customers already shop, not a niche channel), price premium capped modestly (~20–25%), and brand trust transfer from an established name.
**Trade-offs**: skipping distribution/price discipline confines the product to a small "green ghetto" niche regardless of genuine environmental merit.
