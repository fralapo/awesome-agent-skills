# Chapter 3: Pricing, Distribution & Promotion

## Pricing — Cost Structure & Break-Even
- **Profit equation**: Total Revenue − Total Cost = Profit. Total Revenue = Price × Quantity.
- **Cost classifications**: Variable costs vary with production volume (raw materials). Fixed costs don't vary within a relevant range (rent, insurance) but can step up beyond a threshold. Product (manufacturing) costs vs. period (non-manufacturing) costs. Fixed-cost absorption: more units produced/sold means smaller fixed cost per unit — incentivizing volume (deliberately selling below cost to spread fixed costs internationally is illegal "dumping"). Direct costs are traceable to a specific product; indirect costs (a plant manager's salary) require allocation across the product mix.
- **Break-even analysis**: Break-even volume = Fixed Costs ÷ (Price − Unit Variable Cost). Calculate early — even at opportunity-identification stage — to sanity-check whether a target price/volume is achievable relative to total category size (a break-even requirement bigger than the entire addressable category is a fatal red flag).
- **Price elasticity of demand**: elastic (>1, price-sensitive) vs. inelastic (<1, insensitive) vs. unitary (=1). The number of available substitutes is the primary driver — more substitutes = more elastic. Common inelastic examples: prescription medication, gasoline, salt; common elastic examples: apparel, discretionary/sale-driven categories. A genuine demand-curve shift requires competitor prices, consumer income, or preferences to change — not just movement along the curve.

## Pricing — Strategic Approaches
- **Skimming**: introduce at a high price to recoup R&D/advertising investment and capture innovators (a small % of adopters), then lower price on a planned schedule as adoption spreads. Most effective when: lowering price would only marginally affect volume, a high initial price won't attract new competitors, customers read high price as a quality signal, and a segment exists willing to buy immediately at the high price. Skimming can be followed later by penetration pricing (recoup R&D first, then chase volume) — but the reverse sequence rarely works, because customers anchor to a low launch price as the "real" price and resist an increase.
- **Penetration pricing**: introduce at a low price to drive volume/adoption quickly. Requires production capacity sufficient to meet resulting demand — a major risk if capacity is built on assumptions that don't materialize, or a competitor leapfrogs with superior technology first. Price alone doesn't create category-level need awareness.
- **Prestige pricing**: pricing deliberately above functional-equivalent competitors to signal/create luxury and exclusivity (a Rolex vs. a functionally equivalent watch). Retailers price gift items *higher*, not lower, around gifting holidays — "too cheap" undermines suitability as a meaningful gift.
- **Cost-plus / standard markup**: price = cost + a fixed dollar or percentage markup. Standard markup varies dramatically by category (groceries ~8–15%, home furnishings 30–50%, apparel 100–200%). Dominant method in B2B, where the buyer typically knows the seller's approximate cost structure and expects a transparent, reasonable margin — compressing a channel partner's margin too aggressively (5% instead of a fair 15%) undermines their ability to stay in business and threatens the whole relationship; sustainable B2B pricing must be win-win.
- **Profit-oriented (target margin) pricing**: total gross profit dollars, not margin percentage, is what matters — "you can't take percentages to the bank, only dollars." A lower-margin, higher-volume strategy can yield equal or greater absolute profit than a higher-margin, lower-volume strategy; always compare total contribution dollars.
- **Loss-leader pricing**: selling one item at/below cost to drive foot traffic, expecting profit on the broader basket. Manufacturers police this via **MAP (Minimum Advertised Price)** — a floor below which retailers cannot *advertise* — distinct from **MSRP**, a non-binding suggested ceiling.
- **Good-better-best (tiered) pricing**: the same category at multiple price/feature tiers, differentiating specs/design/size across tiers to justify each price point. There's a practical limit to how wide the band can stretch before it damages brand coherence.
- **Magic price points**: certain round-number-adjacent price points ($4.99, $9.99, $19.99) reliably drive disproportionate volume; manufacturers and retailers coordinate markups to land on them.
- **Price-increase disguises**: rather than raising a visible sticker price in a price-sensitive market, sellers shrink quantity/content at the same price, or add promotions (more product free) instead of visibly cutting price — because visible price cuts are hard to reverse. Once a price is publicly reduced, customers anchor to it as the "real" price and resist paying regular price again.
- **Promotions / temporary price reductions — the overstocking risk**: retailers and consumers both overstock on deal, producing a short-term spike immediately followed by a demand trough as inventory is worked down. "Buy one, get one free" delivers similar perceived-value uplift to a price cut without resetting the customer's anchor on the base unit price.
- **Rebates**: influence decisions psychologically even though most buyers never redeem them, because buyers mentally treat the discounted price as the effective price at the point of decision.
- **Channel-specific product differentiation**: when a low-cost retailer can profitably underprice higher-service retailers, differentiate the SKU per channel (unique configuration/packaging/color) so retailers are never directly price-comparable on an identical item — preserves other channel partners' ability to sell at required margin.
- **Freemium vs. paid-only** decision depends on: available cash runway to survive a worst case, and the exit/scale outcome being optimized for (venture-scale favors free/freemium to remove price as an adoption barrier; a lifestyle/cash-flow business favors early paid validation).
- **Anti-pattern — free/freemium as a crutch**: offering free or heavily discounted trials by default can signal weak conviction in the product's value; forcing the business to sell and charge from day one imposes pricing discipline.

## Distribution / Place
- **Channel value chain**: processor → manufacturer → wholesaler/distributor → retailer → end user. Each intermediary performs a real function (wholesalers break bulk case-packs into smaller usable quantities) and earns margin for it — bypassing intermediaries doesn't eliminate the cost of the function, it just relocates who captures the margin.
- **Distribution intensity continuum**: **Intensive** (sell everywhere — convenience goods); **Selective** (a curated subset of channels — shopping goods, higher-price items where channel fit matters; selling a $100 perfume in a discount chain erodes brand equity even if units could be sold there); **Exclusive** (one/few partners, often to elevate perceived value/exclusivity or extract a strategic payment for exclusive rights, e.g., an initial carrier exclusive).
- **Direct vs. indirect vs. dual distribution**: direct = manufacturer sells straight to end customer; indirect = sells through intermediaries; dual = both simultaneously (common in pharma and branded retail).
- **Retailer format taxonomy**: Mass merchants/discounters (EDLP — everyday-low-price, rollbacks instead of promotional cycles); Department stores (High-Low — higher regular prices offset by frequent sales); Wholesale clubs (membership-gated, bulk unit economics); Grocery/drug/convenience stores (increasingly use "scramble merchandising" — carrying non-core items to capture more basket share); Specialty stores (narrow category, deep assortment within it); General merchandisers (broad category, shallower assortment per category — the line blurs over time as assortments broaden).
- **Store-within-a-store / leased department**: a large retailer leases counter/floor space to a brand rather than carrying/owning that inventory itself — reduces the host's inventory risk and staffing while still delivering assortment breadth.
- **Planogram (POG)**: the standardized fixed shelf layout replicated across a chain. Adding a new SKU requires removing another — retail space is zero-sum, evaluated via units/dollar sales/margin dollars/margin % per section over time (e.g., trailing 52 weeks).
- **Adjacencies**: deliberate placement of complementary products next to each other (pasta next to sauce) to increase basket size — continually optimized, not static.
- **Store flow and visual theme**: engineered layouts direct traffic through complementary categories to maximize cross-selling; lighting/color/music/decor are intentionally designed to reinforce brand identity at every touch point.
- **Make-to-stock vs. make-to-order**: make-to-stock = inventory held in anticipation of demand (instant gratification, ties up capital, stockout/obsolescence risk); make-to-order = acquired/produced only after the order (less inventory investment, longer delivery times). Hybrid models combine both.
- **Online vs. physical retail trade-offs**: physical requires far higher startup capital/time but suits high-involvement/trial-dependent/sensory categories (cars, jewelry, perfume, prestige goods); online reduces startup cost and enables customization but faces resistance for products requiring trial, immediacy, or perishability management.
- **Vertical vs. horizontal integration** (see also Ch1): vertical = capturing manufacturing + retail margin by opening owned stores; horizontal = expanding within retailing into new adjacent categories.

## Promotion & Integrated Marketing Communications (IMC)
- **IMC**: marketing communications spans advertising, sales promotion, publicity/PR, personal selling, and direct mail — not advertising alone. All channels/messages must be consistent (look, feel, personality, identity) across every customer touch point, mapped via touch point mapping.
- **Contest vs. sweepstakes**: contest requires demonstrated skill to win; sweepstakes is a random drawing requiring none.
- **Advertising media/vehicles**: broadcast (TV, radio), print (newspapers, magazines), outdoor (billboards, transit, street furniture), internet/digital, direct response (unique 1-800 numbers per medium, to track source).
- **Clutter and sensory overload**: ads must have "stopping power" to break through repeated exposure/frequency, required for the message to be learned and remembered.
- **Media scheduling strategies**: Continuous (year-round, suits steady-purchase products); Pulsing (continuous with increased spend during certain periods); Flighting (alternating on/off periods) — flighting risks losing awareness and share to competitors during off periods ("out of sight, out of mind").
- **Reach vs. frequency**: reach = % of target audience exposed; frequency = average number of exposures. Both drive media cost.
- **Cost per thousand (CPM)** = (Cost of ad × 1,000) ÷ Circulation/Audience size — used to compare vehicles on an apples-to-apples basis (typical US CPM range ~$20–$120, a useful sanity check). When CPMs are equal, the tie-breaker is audience-profile match (least waste), not raw price.
- **Media selection**: match a vehicle's readership demographic to the target audience profile; since no single vehicle achieves a full match, campaigns typically run across ~8–12 vehicles to maximize reach while minimizing waste.
- **Print ad components**: headline (stopping power), body copy, image, and — critically — the logo/brand identity must always be prominent; under-emphasizing brand identity wastes the entire ad spend.
- **Sales promotion tools**: coupons, deals (buy-one-get-one), premiums, rebates, free samples, point-of-purchase (POP) displays — objective is reducing perceived (especially financial) risk, driving trial among light/non-users, generating repeat purchase.
- **Encoding/decoding model**: the marketer encodes a message via creative; the target audience must decode (process, learn, and store the message and brand association in memory) — if audiences can't connect message/imagery to the brand name, the ad investment fails regardless of creative quality.
